## When Budget Equipment Stops Being Enough

Most hobbyist prospectors reach a recognizable inflection point. You've run your entry-level highbanker for a season or two, you understand the mechanics, you know what a good setup looks like — and you're starting to notice what your current unit isn't catching. The riffles aren't quite right. The spray bar has a dead zone on one side. The aluminum flexes under load in ways that change your slope angle mid-run. You're leaving gold behind and you know it.

This is the moment that separates prospectors who stay hobbyists from the ones who get serious. And it's exactly the moment Proline's Mini Highbanker is built for.

## What the 2.5" Mini Highbanker Includes

The Proline 2.5" Mini Highbanker ships with the complete sluice assembly: a 48"–54" long aluminum sluice box, an expanded metal hopper with interchangeable classifier screens, a full-width spray bar, and adjustable legs. The "2.5 inch" designation refers to the suction nozzle diameter — relevant if you choose to upgrade to the suction dredge configuration, but it also indicates the scale of the water system this unit is designed around.

The spray bar is where you'll notice the difference from budget units immediately. A full-width bar with multiple evenly-spaced jets delivers consistent water pressure across the entire hopper. No dead zones. No one side running heavy while the other starves. The water sheet that falls onto your sluice bed is even, predictable, and adjustable via pump pressure.

The hopper's expanded metal screen classifies your material as you shovel. Oversized rocks and debris stay on top; fines and small gravels drop through onto the sluice. Proline's interchangeable screen system lets you swap mesh sizes depending on what you're working — coarser mesh when you're in a hurry, finer mesh when you're processing concentrates or working in an area with small gold.

**The pump isn't included.** Proline's equipment is designed around the Honda WX10 — a 1-inch, 4-stroke pump at about 42 gallons per minute. It's the right tool for this unit. If you already own a compatible pump from a previous setup, you're ready to run. If you're buying new, budget the Honda or an equivalent into your total.

## The Pump-Fed Advantage — Why Highbanking Beats Streamside Sluicing

If you've been running a hand sluice in the creek, you've learned firsthand how variable stream current is. One stretch of water has enough gradient to push material cleanly through your box. Fifty yards upstream, the water slows and your riffles fill with gravel. You spend as much time repositioning as you do prospecting.

A pump-fed highbanker removes that dependency entirely. The pump dictates the water velocity in your sluice box, not the creek. You can run the exact flow rate your material needs regardless of stream conditions. Shallow, slow late-summer creeks that would shut down a hand sluice are fully workable. Remote bench deposits above the current waterline — where nobody running a creek sluice can reach — become accessible. Dry gravel bars become processing areas. You bring the water to the gold instead of bringing the gold to the water.

The Proline's full-width spray bar is a critical part of why this advantage is maximized. With budget spray bars that don't distribute evenly, you still end up with inconsistent water flow and recovery gaps. Proline's bar gives you control over the full width of the sluice — that's what "pump-fed" is supposed to feel like.

## Field Setup and Operation

Proline units are designed for practical deployment. The adjustable legs let you set your slope angle on uneven terrain — typically 4–7 degrees of fall is the working range for most placer material. Heavy clay-bound material wants less slope and more water; coarse, clean gravel can handle more angle.

Set up takes roughly 20–30 minutes on a new site: deploy and level the unit, connect the pump discharge to the spray bar inlet, run the pump inlet hose to the creek, prime and start the pump, and check your water distribution before you feed material. Run clean water through the box first and watch how the sheet falls. If it's even across the full hopper width, you're ready.

Feeding the hopper is the rhythm that dictates your throughput. Load steadily without overheating the classifier screen. Clay-bound gravels may need breaking up by hand or a spray from a second hose before they'll pass through the mesh. In clean, loose gravel, you'll find yourself shoveling at a pace that keeps pace easily with the unit's capacity.

At the end of a run, recovery is a clean process. Shut off the pump, let the water drain, then carefully collect the material trapped in the riffles and miner's moss. This is your concentrate — a fraction of the total volume you shoveled, loaded with heavies. Pan it out, and you'll see what the day produced.

## The Modular Upgrade Path

One of the Proline Mini Highbanker's distinguishing features is what it can become. The 2.5" designation aligns the water system with Proline's suction dredge components. If your prospecting evolves and you want to start working creek-bed material underwater — not just bank and bench deposits — you can add a suction nozzle, jet assembly, and float system to convert this unit to a suction dredge configuration.

That modularity is meaningful. You're not buying a dead-end unit. You're buying the foundation of a system that scales with your skills and ambitions. When you're ready to work underwater, the investment you made in the highbanker carries forward rather than sitting idle.

**A critical note on suction dredging:** Before you consider any suction dredge upgrade, check your state's regulations. Suction dredges are banned or severely restricted in California, Washington, Oregon, and parts of other states. The highbanker configuration itself is broadly legal — it's the suction mode that triggers restrictions. Know your state before you spend money on the suction upgrade.

## Comparing Proline Against the Field

**vs. ASR Outdoor (~$350):** The ASR is the right starting point when you're learning. The Proline is the right unit when you know what you're doing and you're tired of learning the limits of budget aluminum. The Proline's heavier construction, better spray bar, and manufacturer support justify the extra $150 for anyone running more than a few trips per year.

**vs. Keene A52S (~$800):** This is the closest and most important comparison. The Keene is a wider, longer unit with a refined riffle system that Keene has been improving since the 1950s. If you're running high-volume material or chasing very fine gold, the A52S's sluice box geometry gives it an edge. The Proline's advantage is the modular upgrade path — you can grow it into a full dredge setup; the Keene A52S is a highbanker-only unit. Budget-to-budget, the Proline and Keene A52S are both serious choices at similar price points. Your decision comes down to whether you plan to eventually add suction capability.
