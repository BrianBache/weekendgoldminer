## Why You're Done With the Hand Sluice

There's a ceiling to what a hand sluice can do, and most hobbyist prospectors hit it around trip three or four. The problem isn't the sluice itself — a well-run hand sluice is a legitimate gold-recovery tool. The problem is the water. To run a hand sluice, you need a section of creek with enough gradient and flow to push material through the box at the right velocity. Too slow, and the heavies settle but the box fills with gravel. Too fast, and everything — including your gold — washes off the end.

You spend half your time auditioning stream locations instead of processing material. And when you find a gravel bar loaded with potential, you've got no way to work it because it's twenty feet from the water's edge.

A highbanker solves this entirely. You bring the water to the material, not the other way around.

## What a Highbanker Actually Does

A highbanker is a pump-fed sluice system. A water pump pulls from the creek and pushes flow through a spray bar mounted above a hopper. The hopper — a classifier screen built into the top of the unit — screens out oversized material as you shovel. Fines and small gravels drop through the hopper and into the sluice box below, carried by a controlled sheet of water across the riffle system. Gold, being dense, sinks to the bottom of the riffles and gets trapped. Lighter gravels wash off the end.

The pump gives you consistent, adjustable water pressure regardless of stream conditions. You can position the highbanker on a dry gravel bar, a bench deposit above the creek, or any flat-ish surface within hose range of water. That flexibility opens up a huge range of deposits that are physically inaccessible to hand sluice users.

The ASR Outdoor Deluxe Highbanker brings this capability to the entry-level price point — and for a lot of weekend prospectors, that's exactly what they need.

## What You Get

The ASR Outdoor Deluxe Highbanker ships as an assembled unit with the following components: an aluminum sluice box (approximately 30–50 inches long, depending on the specific model year), an expanded metal hopper with a built-in classifier screen, a spray bar water manifold mounted above the hopper, and adjustable aluminum legs. The riffles inside the sluice box — typically Hungarian-style aluminum riffles — sit on top of rubber matting or miner's moss to catch fine particles.

The hopper's expanded metal screen is typically 1/4" or 1/2" mesh. As you shovel material onto the hopper, oversized rocks and debris stay on top for you to inspect and discard. Gravels, sand, and fines drop through onto the sluice below. The spray bar jets water across the hopper to help break up clay-bound material and wash fines down into the box.

**What it doesn't include:** a pump. You'll need a 1" to 1.5" water pump rated for at least 110–200 gallons per hour — and honestly, more is better. The Honda WX10 is the gold standard pairing for this unit (see our separate review), but any reliable 1" pump will work. Budget another $150–$400 depending on your pump choice when you're calculating total setup cost.

## The Pump-Fed Advantage in Practice

Here's what changes when you switch from a hand sluice to a highbanker setup like this one.

First, you stop worrying about current. On a slow summer creek where the water's barely moving, your hand sluice is a decoration. Pump-fed, that same location becomes fully workable — you dial up pressure on the pump and run the box at the velocity you choose, not the velocity the creek gives you.

Second, you can work bench deposits. Those ancient river gravels sitting above the current water line, weathered and loaded with accumulations from paleo-channels? They're where a lot of the best placer gold hides. With a highbanker, you can position the unit on the bench, run a hose down to the creek, and work material that nobody dragging a hand sluice can touch.

Third, throughput increases substantially. With consistent water pressure and a wider hopper, you're shoveling more material per hour than you'd process in a hand sluice. The classification happens automatically as you shovel — you're not stopping to classify separately. Feed the hopper, keep it moving.

## Build Quality — Honest Assessment

The ASR Outdoor Deluxe is a budget unit. That's not a disqualification — it's a description of what you're getting. The aluminum is lighter gauge than you'll find on a Keene or Proline, which means it handles normal weekend use well but shows wear faster under heavy seasonal use. The welds are functional, the riffles are reasonably consistent, and the spray bar works as designed.

The riffle system is the area where budget shows most clearly. On a professional-grade highbanker, the riffles are designed with precise geometry that creates turbulence at exactly the right intensity to separate gold from lighter material without flushing fine particles. The ASR's riffles will catch your flake gold and coarse material — but in areas with fine or flour gold, you may lose some material that a more refined riffle system would trap. This is worth knowing before you buy.

The adjustable legs are a genuine plus. Being able to set the slope angle — typically 3–7 degrees of fall is ideal for a sluice — lets you optimize recovery for different material types. Steeper slope for coarse material, shallower for fine gold. Having the adjustability matters more than it sounds when you're working a new site and dialing in your setup.

## Setting Up the ASR in the Field

The setup process is straightforward. Level your ground as best you can, deploy the legs, and adjust until you've got roughly 5 degrees of slope on the sluice. Connect your pump's discharge hose to the spray bar inlet — you may need a hose adapter depending on your pump's discharge diameter. Run the pump inlet hose into the creek, prime the pump, and start your flow.

Before you feed material, run clean water through the box for a few minutes and check the water distribution across the hopper. You want an even sheet of water reaching all areas of the classifier screen, not just one side. If your spray bar has multiple jets, make sure none are clogged. Adjust pump pressure if your model allows it.

When you're satisfied with the flow pattern, start shoveling onto the hopper. Load it steadily — don't heap it — and let the screen work. Use a hand or a brush to work clay-bound material through the classifier screen if it's binding up. The goal is a consistent feed of fines dropping into the sluice box at a rate the water can process without swamping the riffles.

## Who Should Buy This

The ASR Outdoor Deluxe Highbanker is the right choice if you're making your first move into pump-fed prospecting. You want to understand how highbanker sluicing works, you're not ready to spend $700+ on a Keene, and you want something that ships from Amazon with a return window if it doesn't work out. For casual to moderate weekend use in areas with coarse-to-medium placer gold, this unit does its job.

It's not the right choice if you're chasing fine or flour gold — the riffle system isn't refined enough to reliably catch it. It's also not the choice for prospectors running heavy seasonal schedules who'll wear through lighter aluminum construction. And it's not the choice if you want manufacturer support and parts availability years from now — specialty mining brands like Keene and Proline support their equipment long-term in a way that Amazon-sourced brands generally don't.

## Comparing Your Options

At roughly $350, the ASR Outdoor is significantly cheaper than the Keene A52S at ~$800. That $450 difference is real and it matters — but so is the gap in build quality and riffle engineering. If you're serious about recovering fine gold and you're going out more than a handful of times per year, the Keene will pay for itself in better recovery over time.

Going the other direction: a basic hand sluice at $60 is the smart choice if you're not ready to invest in a pump. Hand sluicing is a legitimate technique and teaches you more about water and material behavior than any other method. Master the hand sluice first, then graduate to the highbanker when you know what you're looking for.

The ASR lives in the middle — not as versatile as the entry-level hand sluice in terms of simplicity, not as capable as the Keene in terms of recovery, but the best way to learn pump-fed sluicing without a significant financial commitment.

## The Bottom Line

The ASR Outdoor Deluxe Highbanker opens the door to one of the most productive methods in hobbyist placer mining. Pump-fed sluicing frees you from stream current, lets you work deposits that hand sluice users can't reach, and dramatically increases your material throughput per day. The ASR delivers that capability at a price point that makes sense for someone who's ready to step up but isn't ready to make the full jump to professional-grade equipment.

Just remember: factor the pump into your budget before you buy. The unit itself doesn't do anything without water pressure behind it, and a cheap pump that dies on the creek is worse than no pump at all. Pair this with a reliable 1" pump, learn your setup, and you'll be pulling material from spots nobody with a hand sluice can touch.
