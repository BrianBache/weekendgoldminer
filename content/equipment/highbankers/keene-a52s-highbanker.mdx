## What Sets the A52S Apart — The Sluice Box

The "A52" in the model name refers to the sluice box dimensions: 52 inches long and 10 inches wide. That's the first thing that separates this unit from budget competitors. More box length means a longer flow path for material, which gives heavies more time to settle into the riffle system before they reach the end. More box width means more material can be processed per unit of water — you're not artificially narrowing your throughput.

Serious prospectors think about sluice box dimensions the way woodworkers think about table saw capacity. It's not just a spec number — it's how much work the unit can do. A 52-inch box at 10 inches wide processes meaningfully more material per hour than a 36-inch box at 8 inches wide, and the difference compounds across a full day of prospecting.

## The Riffle System — Where Recovery Happens

The A52S uses Keene's Hungarian drop-riffle system — angled aluminum riffles oriented to create turbulent pockets immediately behind each bar. As water and material flow across the sluice, the riffle geometry generates low-pressure zones on the downstream face of each bar. Dense particles — gold, black sand, heavy minerals — drop into those pockets and stay. Lighter material rides the water current over the top and continues toward the end of the box.

Behind and beneath the riffles, Keene runs miner's moss — an open-cell matting with fine fibers that catch particles the riffle system nudges toward the bottom but doesn't fully trap. Fine gold, flour gold, and heavy black sand all concentrate in the moss. When you recover at the end of a run, the moss holds a significant portion of your fine gold that would have washed off the end of a less refined system.

The riffle system is adjustable. You can swap riffle configurations based on what you're working — coarser riffles for faster flow and coarser material, finer setups when you're running concentrates or working in an area with small grain gold. Keene sells replacement and alternative riffle components separately, which means you can tune the A52S to specific conditions rather than accepting a one-size-fits-all setup.

## The Pump-Fed Advantage — Control That Cheaper Units Can't Match

Understanding why a highbanker beats a hand sluice is understanding why water control is everything in placer mining.

A hand sluice borrows its motive force from the creek. The creek's gradient determines your water velocity. The season determines the flow volume. Neither of these is under your control — you work with what the creek gives you, set up where the current is strong enough to push your material, and move on when conditions change. Fine-gold areas with slow current are effectively off-limits. Dry season? Half your spots disappear.

A pump-fed highbanker gives you the water velocity you choose, at the pressure you set, at any location within hose-length of a water source. The A52S paired with a Honda WX10 delivers consistent flow that you calibrate to your material. Running coarse gravel? Open up the pressure. Working fine gold concentrates? Dial it back for lower turbulence. The pump is your throttle, and the A52S's spray bar distributes that controlled pressure evenly across the full hopper width.

That control is worth more than the $800 price tag implies. When you're getting maximum performance out of a 52-inch riffle system with perfectly even water distribution and properly adjusted slope, you're running a prospecting operation — not just sloshing gravel around.

## Setting Up on the Creek

The A52S deploys in 20–30 minutes once you've done it a few times. The adjustable legs give you the slope control you need on irregular terrain — aim for 4–6 degrees of fall as your starting point and adjust based on how material moves through the box. Coarser material and higher water pressure want more angle. Fine material and slower flow want less.

Connect the pump discharge hose to the spray bar inlet — a 1-inch hose connector is standard — and run the pump inlet hose into the creek. Position the pump intake where it draws clear water, not stirred-up sediment from your digging area. Prime the pump, start it up, and check your spray bar distribution before you feed a single shovel of material. Watch the water sheet fall across the hopper. It should reach all areas of the classifier screen without pooling or running heavy on one side. If it doesn't, check for clogged jets.

Once you're satisfied with the distribution, start feeding material steadily. Don't overload the hopper. Consistent feeding at a pace the water can process — rather than occasional heavy loads — maximizes the time material spends in the flow path and gives the riffles the best chance to do their work.

## Recovery — The Moment of Truth

After a productive run, recovery is where you find out what the A52S caught. Shut off the pump and let water drain from the box. Working from the discharge end upward, carefully remove the riffle sections and lay them in a wash tub or bucket of water. Rinse the concentrates that have built up behind each riffle into the tub. Then remove the miner's moss and rinse it thoroughly — the moss holds a surprising amount of fine material.

Pan out the combined concentrates. The A52S's recovery will show you not just your coarse gold but the fine material that cheaper units wash past. If you've been running a budget highbanker and switching to the A52S, the difference in fine gold retention is usually visible in your first recovery.
