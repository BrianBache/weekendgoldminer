## ⚠️ State Restrictions — Read Before You Go Any Further

Suction dredge mining is one of the most tightly regulated activities in recreational prospecting. Before you read another word about what this machine can do, you need to know where it's illegal to use it.

**Suction dredges are BANNED or severely restricted in:**

- **California** — Statewide moratorium on suction dredge mining has been in effect and repeatedly extended. Operating a suction dredge in California is illegal in virtually all waterways. Penalties include significant fines and equipment seizure. This applies regardless of the size of the dredge.
- **Washington** — Restrictions apply to most salmon-bearing waterways. In practice, this covers the majority of productive prospecting creeks in the state. Permit requirements vary by county and change year to year. Many locations are effectively permanently closed.
- **Oregon** — Extensive closures across the state. Where suction dredging is permitted, an Oregon Department of State Lands permit is required. Many historically productive locations are off-limits.
- **Montana** — State-specific restrictions apply. Seasonal windows exist in some waterways; others are permanently closed. Research every specific location before operations.
- **Idaho** — Similar to Montana — waterway-specific and seasonal. Not a blanket ban, but thorough research required for each site.
- **Other states** — Regulations are not static. States that allowed dredging last year may have restricted it this year. Environmental litigation and seasonal salmon protection measures can change the landscape on short notice.

**What to do:** Contact your state's fish and wildlife agency directly. Look up your specific waterway by name. Verify with the local district ranger if you're in a national forest. Cross-reference the Western Mining Alliance's current regulation database. Do not assume that what was legal last season is legal now.

If you're in California, Washington, or Oregon: this review is primarily informational for you. The machine described below cannot legally operate in suction mode in most of your state's waterways. The Keene A52S highbanker or the Dredge/Highbanker Combo in highbanker-only mode may be better choices for your situation.

For prospectors in states where suction dredging remains legal and you've verified your specific waterway: here's what the Keene 2" will do for you.

## What Suction Dredging Actually Accesses

Surface prospecting — panning, sluicing, highbanking — works on material you can shovel. Gravel bars, bench deposits, bank material, overburden. These are legitimate gold-bearing targets, and surface methods can produce real color on the right ground.

But the creek bed itself is where placer gold has been accumulating for centuries. Running water in a stream acts as a continuous natural sluice. Over time, heavy particles — gold being among the heaviest — work their way down through gravel and sand to bedrock. They collect in bedrock crevices, behind boulders, in potholes worn into the rock. A layer of gravel sitting on top of bedrock, sometimes called false bedrock, can hold concentrations that make the material above it look pale by comparison.

None of this is reachable from the surface. A shovel can't excavate the bottom of a 3-foot-deep creek. A highbanker can't process underwater material. To work creek-bed gold, you need a suction dredge — and that's the argument for this machine.

## How the Keene 2" Works

The operating principle is the venturi effect. Your engine drives a water pump that pushes high-velocity water through a jet nozzle inside the suction assembly. High-velocity water through a constricted opening creates a low-pressure zone immediately downstream — the same principle that makes a carburetor atomize fuel. That low-pressure zone pulls creek-bed material — water, gravel, sand, gold — up through a 2-inch suction hose and delivers it to the sluice box mounted on the surface.

Keene's jet dredge design is respected specifically for its suction efficiency. More suction per unit of pump output means more material moved per hour of engine time and per gallon of fuel. In remote locations where fuel is limited, that efficiency compounds across a full day of operations.

The sluice box on a 2" dredge is typically 36–42 inches long, mounted on a float system — either inflatable pontoons or foam floats — that keeps the box level on the water surface. Dredged material enters the top of the sluice, water and light material wash off the end into the creek, and heavies settle into the riffles and miner's moss on the sluice bottom. Gold stays in the box. Tailings go back to the creek.

## Portability — The 2" Advantage

The 2" is the smallest practical dredge in Keene's lineup, and that sizing decision matters for access. A complete 2" setup — engine, pump, sluice box, floats, hoses, suction nozzle — typically weighs 40–60 pounds. Heavy for a day hike, but manageable in pieces: the engine and pump as one carry, the sluice and float assembly as another, hoses and accessories as a third.

This is a setup you can get to locations that a 4" dredge simply can't reach. Narrow canyon creeks, steep bank access, trails that don't allow for wheeled equipment — the 2" goes places larger dredges don't. For prospectors who want to work virgin or lightly worked ground rather than the same accessible bars everyone hits, that portability is genuine competitive advantage.

Setup takes 20–30 minutes for an experienced operator. First deployment on a new site takes longer as you figure out float positioning, suction hose routing, and tailings placement. By your third or fourth time running the same spot, you'll have a system.

## Running the Dredge — What the Work Looks Like

Dredging is physically different from highbanking. You're in the water — or underwater, working with a mask — maneuvering the suction nozzle over the creek bed. In most productive dredge operations, the operator positions themselves downstream of the suction hose and works the nozzle forward into material, letting the venturi pull gravel and water up through the hose.

Bedrock crevices are where the gold concentrates. After clearing the overburden gravel off a bedrock section, experienced dredgers use a crevice tool — a narrow suction nozzle or hand pick — to work material out of cracks and depressions. This is painstaking work, but it's where the highest concentrations typically live.

Water temperature is a practical constraint. Most productive placer creeks run cold even in summer — 45–58°F in many western mountain streams. For extended underwater work, a wetsuit or drysuit is not optional; it's a safety and comfort requirement. Factor this into your equipment budget.

## Throughput Expectations — Honest Numbers

A 2" dredge in clean, loose gravel on productive ground can move 0.5–1.5 cubic yards per hour. That's the honest range accounting for working around boulders, clearing overburden, crevice work, and normal field conditions. In tight bedrock crevices — which is where you want to spend your time — actual throughput will be lower because you're working precisely, not just shoveling.

A 2.5" dredge moves more volume. A 3" dredge moves significantly more. But for most hobbyist operators prospecting on weekends, the 2" is not the production bottleneck — time is. You'll work a creek section thoroughly at 2" before you exhaust it. Moving up to larger diameter suction makes sense when you're consistently running out of productive material to process, not before.

## Building Your Full Setup

Beyond the dredge itself, a complete operational setup requires:

- **Wetsuit or drysuit** — not optional in cold water; $100–$600 depending on type and quality
- **Mask and snorkel or full face mask** — for working underwater; $30–$150
- **Crevice tools** — suction nozzle attachments, hand picks; $20–$80
- **Fuel** — for field runs; carry more than you think you need
- **Recovery pan** — to process sluice concentrates at the end of the run
- **Valid prospecting claims or legal right to work the specific waterway**

Total all-in for a new setup: expect $2,000–$2,500 when you add personal protective equipment and accessories to the dredge and engine cost.
