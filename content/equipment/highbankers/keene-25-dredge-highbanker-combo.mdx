## ⚠️ Read This Before You Buy

Before we talk about what this machine can do, you need to know where it can't do it.

**The suction dredge function of this unit is banned or severely restricted in the following states:**

- **California** — statewide moratorium on suction dredge mining, indefinitely extended. Operating a suction dredge in California can result in significant fines and equipment seizure.
- **Washington** — restrictions apply to most salmon-bearing streams; permit requirements vary by county and change year to year. Many practical locations are effectively closed.
- **Oregon** — significant closures, permit required where suction dredging is allowed. Many waterways are off-limits.
- **Montana and Idaho** — seasonal windows and stream-specific restrictions apply. Not a blanket ban, but research required for every specific location.
- **Other states** — regulations change. What was legal last season may not be this season. Always verify with your state's fish and wildlife agency before operating in suction dredge mode.

The highbanker configuration of this unit is broadly legal in most states. The suction dredge configuration is where you must do your homework. If you're in California, Washington, or Oregon, this unit's core capability is legally inaccessible to you in most waterways — factor that into your purchase decision.

If you're in a state where suction dredging is legal and you've verified your specific waterway, the rest of this review is for you. This is a remarkable piece of equipment.

## The Concept: One Setup, Two Prospecting Modes

The Keene 2.5" Dredge/Highbanker Combo solves a problem that serious hobbyist prospectors eventually run into: gold doesn't care about the bank.

You can work a gravel bar all day and recover real gold from bench deposits and bank material. But underneath the creek bed — in the bedrock crevices and false bedrock layers that the current has swept clean for centuries — that's where placer gold concentrates in volumes that no amount of surface bank mining can match. Working that material requires getting underwater, and getting underwater requires suction.

Most hobbyists own one or the other: a highbanker for bank work, or a dredge for creek work. The Keene combo eliminates that choice. You run it as a highbanker when you're working bank deposits, convert it to suction dredge mode when you want to work the creek bed, and do all of it with the same motor, pump, and sluice box.

That versatility is the entire argument for this unit's price premium.

## How the System Works

In highbanker mode, the setup is familiar: pump feeds the spray bar above the hopper, you shovel material from the bank onto the classifier screen, fines drop through onto the sluice, gold settles into the riffles and miner's moss. The A52-class sluice box — approximately 48–54 inches long — handles the recovery. Everything works exactly as you'd expect from Keene's established highbanker design.

In dredge mode, the configuration changes. A 2.5-inch suction hose connects to a venturi jet nozzle powered by the water pump. The jet nozzle uses high-velocity water to create suction — the same principle as a carburetor, scaled up to move creek gravel. You hold the nozzle underwater, maneuver it over the creek bed, and gravel, sand, and water are pulled up through the suction hose and into the top of the sluice box. From there, gravity and water flow take over: material passes through the sluice, gold settles, tailings wash off the end.

Keene's jet dredge system is well-regarded specifically for its suction efficiency. The venturi design extracts more suction per unit of pump output than competing designs, which means you're moving more creek bed material per gallon of fuel burned. In a remote location where you're carrying extra gasoline, that efficiency matters.

## The Engine and Pump Question

The Keene 2.5" Combo is designed around a Honda GX series engine — typically a GX80 to GX160 depending on configuration. Honda GX engines are the gold standard in portable power for a reason: they start reliably, run clean, and with proper maintenance will outlast the equipment they're attached to by decades.

Keene offers the combo with and without power unit. If you're buying complete, you'll typically get the engine and pump integrated with the frame. If you're buying the sluice/dredge components only to pair with an existing pump, confirm compatibility before ordering — the jet system is designed around specific flow and pressure specifications.

Total turnkey cost — unit, engine, all accessories — will typically land between $2,000 and $2,500 depending on current pricing and engine choice. That's the honest all-in number to plan around.

## Why Pump-Fed Beats Stream-Dependent Every Time

In dredge mode, you're clearly pump-fed — there's no other option when you're working underwater. But in highbanker mode, the pump-fed advantage over a hand sluice is equally important.

A hand sluice relies on the creek's current for motive force. You're limited to locations where the water moves fast enough to power your box, and the creek determines your water velocity rather than you. In late summer when flows drop, many hand sluice setups become unusable. The highbanker function of this combo runs independently of creek conditions. Slow creek, fast creek, early season melt, late season trickle — the pump creates the flow you need at the pressure you set.

When you operate the full combo, you're working both sides of the prospecting equation simultaneously. Run the suction nozzle over the creek bed to pull material up through the sluice box. When the tide turns and you want to work the bank, shut off the suction nozzle and switch to shovel feeding through the hopper. One unit, all the deposits, same day.

## Setting Up and Breaking Down

The combo setup takes longer than a standalone highbanker — plan 45–60 minutes for a first deployment on a new site. The dredge frame, floats, suction components, hose connections, and sluice positioning all require attention before you can run in dredge mode. The highbanker configuration sets up faster since you skip the float and suction assembly.

For breakdown, reverse the process. The components are modular and labeled, and experienced operators get faster with repetition. Many prospectors stage their setup at camp the night before so they're on the water at first light.

Weight is a real consideration. The complete dredge configuration — frame, sluice, engine, pump, hoses, floats — runs 50–80 pounds. This is not a setup you hike into a remote location for a day trip. Plan for vehicle access to your site, or build the logistics around short carries on a game cart.

## Who This Unit Is For

The Keene 2.5" Combo is for the prospector who has been at this for a while, knows their target areas, has done the legal research, and is ready to work the creek bed seriously. If you've been running a highbanker for a season or two and you keep looking at the creek bed wondering what's down there — this is the unit that answers that question.

It's not for beginners. The investment is substantial, the legal landscape is complicated, and getting full value out of the unit requires knowing enough about prospecting to work both configurations effectively. Start with a highbanker, learn the craft, then step up when you're ready.

It's also not the right unit if your state prohibits suction dredging. In that case, the Keene A52S gives you everything this unit does in highbanker mode at half the price, without the weight and complexity of the dredge components.
