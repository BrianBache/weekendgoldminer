## The Problem With Conventional Riffles

If you've been running a highbanker or sluice box for any length of time, you've noticed something about the concentrates you recover: the gold visible in the pan after cleanup tends to be flake gold and coarser color. It catches well in Hungarian riffles. It's satisfying to see. But ask any experienced prospector about flour gold — the ultra-fine particles so small they're nearly invisible to the naked eye — and you'll hear a consistent story: it moves through conventional riffle systems.

The physics are simple. Flour gold particles are so small that their surface-area-to-mass ratio makes them behave less like dense particles and more like suspended material. In a conventional sluice with turbulent water and fixed aluminum riffles, flour gold gets caught in the general water movement and washed off the end of the box along with the tailings. You'll see it glinting in a tailings check pan and wonder how much you've been letting go all season.

The Gold Cube 3-Stack is built to catch what your sluice misses.

## What Makes the Vortex Tray Design Different

The Gold Cube's trays don't look like anything in conventional sluicing. Instead of riffles — perpendicular bars that create turbulent catch zones — the Gold Cube uses an injection-molded tray with a wave/vortex surface pattern. The geometry of those waves creates a specific type of flow: water and material move across the tray in a controlled vortex pattern that stratifies particles by density rather than simply slowing them down.

In a conventional riffle, material slows when it hits the upstream face of the bar and heavy particles settle. The Gold Cube's vortex motion continuously works material across the tray surface in a spiraling pattern, which gives ultra-fine heavy particles — flour gold, fine platinum, heavy black sand — more opportunities to find their way to the bottom of the tray and stay there. Lighter material rides the vortex to the discharge end and exits.

The three-tray stack adds multiple passes through this process. Material enters the top tray, exits into the second, exits into the third. By the time it's done, material that survived one tray's vortex faces the same process again on the next. It's a staged recovery system in a single unit.

## Where the Gold Cube Fits in Your Workflow

The most common use of the Gold Cube in hobbyist prospecting is as a secondary concentrator. You run your highbanker or sluice box for a full day's session, recover the concentrates from your riffles and miner's moss, and then run those concentrates through the Gold Cube before final panning.

This two-stage approach makes sense for several reasons. Your primary highbanker processes bulk material efficiently — it's optimized for throughput on volume. The Gold Cube handles the fine material that the highbanker concentrates for you, working at lower volume but higher precision. You're not trying to run raw creek gravel through the Gold Cube — that would be underselling its design. You're running the pre-concentrated fines where your fine gold is most likely to be hiding.

The result is a cleanup process where your final panning pan is much smaller in volume and much richer in heavy particles — including the flour gold that your riffle system concentrated but couldn't fully trap.

The Gold Cube also functions as a standalone primary concentrator if you're working bucket material. Dig material, classify it to remove oversized rocks, and feed the fines directly through the Gold Cube with a controlled water source. At up to 200 pounds of material per hour under ideal conditions, the throughput is lower than a highbanker but respectable for targeted work on known productive material.

## Water Requirements — A Real Advantage

One of the Gold Cube's underappreciated strengths is how little water it needs. Conventional highbankers and sluice boxes require significant flow — 60–120 gallons per minute or more depending on the size of the unit. The Gold Cube runs on 5–10 gallons per minute. That's garden hose territory.

The practical implications: you can run the Gold Cube with a small 12-volt recirculating pump, eliminating the need for a gas-powered pump entirely in some use cases. At camp, in the back of a truck, or at a site where noise and fuel are constraints, the Gold Cube operates quietly on minimal power. Some prospectors use a simple gravity feed from a bucket elevated above the unit — no pump at all.

This makes the Gold Cube a viable tool in situations where your Honda WX10 is already running the highbanker and you want to simultaneously process concentrates from a previous run without adding a second gas-powered pump to the setup.

## The Pump-Fed and Non-Pump Setup Options

Unlike a highbanker — which is inherently pump-dependent — the Gold Cube is flexible in its water source:

**Recirculating pump:** A small 12V submersible pump in a 5-gallon bucket of water feeds the unit continuously. Water exits the Gold Cube back into the bucket. This setup is self-contained and works anywhere. It's quiet and fuel-free.

**Garden hose:** If you have hose access at your site, direct connection works. Control the flow with a ball valve at the intake to dial in the right volume.

**Gravity feed:** Elevate a water reservoir above the Gold Cube's inlet and let gravity do the work. Works for low-volume processing on flat terrain.

**Pump-fed from your highbanker pump:** Tee off your existing pump line and run a secondary feed to the Gold Cube. This requires a pump with enough capacity for both units — confirm your WX10's output can cover the combined demand.

## Setting Up and Dialing In

The Gold Cube's learning curve is real but manageable. The unit must be level — even a slight tilt changes the vortex pattern and degrades recovery. Take time with your setup: use a small spirit level on the top tray before you run a drop of water.

Feed rate matters as much as water flow. Too much material entering the top tray at once overwhelms the vortex pattern and flushes heavies out with the discharge. Feed in small, steady amounts — not heaps. Experienced Gold Cube operators develop a rhythm: a steady, light feed that keeps the trays active without overloading them.

Water flow affects stratification. Too little water and material doesn't move across the tray efficiently. Too much and the vortex pattern degrades into simple washdown. The 5–10 GPM range is your working window; within that range, adjust based on how your specific material behaves. Run a test with known material before you commit a full session's concentrates.

## What It Won't Do

The Gold Cube is not a replacement for a highbanker. If you're expecting to eliminate your pump setup and sluice box in favor of the Gold Cube, recalibrate. The throughput simply doesn't match — a highbanker moves material at a pace the Gold Cube can't match on raw creek gravel.

The Gold Cube also requires more setup discipline than a conventional sluice. Level it wrong and you lose material. Feed it too fast and you flush gold. The unit rewards patience and attention. Prospectors who want to shovel aggressively and check the results later will find it frustrating; prospectors who enjoy the methodical, precise end of recovery work will find it satisfying.

Fine gold recovery claims should also be validated against your specific material. The Gold Cube's design is optimized for ultra-fine gold recovery, but "flour gold" behavior varies with particle shape, density, and the specific mineral matrix you're working. Test it on a known sample before drawing conclusions about its performance on your ground.
