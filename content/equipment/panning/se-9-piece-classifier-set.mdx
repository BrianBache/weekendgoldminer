## Why Classification Changes Everything

If you've been panning without classifying first, you've been doing it the hard way. Dropping a shovel's worth of raw creek gravel straight into your pan and working it down is slow, exhausting, and — here's the part that stings — it actually washes gold out. When you're tumbling big chunks of rock and pebble around in your pan, the turbulence that lifts and sorts material doesn't distinguish between quartz and color. Fine gold gets caught in the churn and lost over the lip before you ever see it.

Classification solves this by sorting your material before it ever hits the pan. You screen out the big stuff, discard the oversize, and pan only the concentrated fines. It's the difference between working a shovel at a time and working a bucket at a time efficiently. Serious prospectors classify obsessively. The SE 9-Piece Classifier Set gives you the full system to do it right.

## What's in the Set

The nine classifiers span a range that covers every scenario you'll encounter in the field. At the top end, the 1/2-inch screen separates out large rocks and debris — the junk you'd otherwise be breaking your wrists trying to pan through. Working down through 1/4", 1/8", 1/12", 1/30", 1/50", and on to the finest 1/100" mesh, you have the ability to isolate increasingly fine material for increasingly focused recovery work.

Each classifier is a circular plastic frame — roughly 13.5 inches in diameter — with the appropriate mesh screen stretched across the bottom. They're designed to nest on top of a standard 5-gallon bucket. You set one on the bucket, shovel material in, work it around with your hands or a quick shake, and the undersize drops through into the bucket below. Swap to a finer classifier on a second bucket and repeat. By the time you sit down to pan, you're working a fraction of the volume you started with, and that fraction is loaded with the fine material where placer gold actually concentrates.

## Build Quality and Field Durability

The frames are injection-molded plastic — not premium, not flimsy. The mesh is pressed and crimped into place, and under normal field use it holds up well. Reviewers with years of use on these report the mesh staying tight. The weak point, if there is one, is leaving these in a cold car overnight in deep winter. Repeated hard freezes can make the plastic brittle and stress the mesh crimp points. If you're prospecting in shoulder-season cold, bring them inside.

Color coding across the nine frames helps in the field. When you've got classifiers spread around a gravel bar and your hands are muddy, being able to grab the green one versus the red one matters more than it sounds. SE didn't have to add color differentiation, but they did, and it's a small design decision that pays off in actual use.

## How to Work the Full Nine

You don't need all nine classifiers every time you go out. The way most prospectors build their workflow is to pick two or three sizes appropriate to the material they're working and the gold size they're targeting. In a coarse placer area with visible flake gold, the 1/2", 1/8", and 1/30" combination covers your bases. In a fine-gold area where you're chasing flour gold, you'll work progressively finer until you're down to the 1/50" or 1/100" screens.

The beauty of having all nine is that you can adapt on the fly. You get to the creek and the material is finer than expected? No problem — reach for a finer screen. You're sampling a new area and want to understand what size gold is present? Run the same sample through progressively finer meshes and see where the gold concentrates. Having the full spectrum is a prospecting education in itself.

The classifiers also work well with a sluice box upstream. Run your material through a coarse classifier first, shovel the undersize through your sluice, then use the finer classifiers to process the sluice concentrates before final panning. The 9-piece set plugs into every stage of that workflow.
