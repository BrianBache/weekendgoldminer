## The Problem with Building a Kit Piece by Piece

Here's what typically happens to a new prospector who decides to buy gear incrementally: they pick up a pan, get to the creek, and realize they have nothing to sort the gravel with. They improvise with a kitchen strainer. They find color, can't pick it up, and spend ten minutes chasing a flake of gold around a wet pan with a fingertip. They drive home with their gold swimming in a zip-lock bag because they didn't bring a vial. They order classifiers when they get home, realize they need tweezers, order those separately, then notice they should have gotten a snuffer bottle too.

A week later they've spent twice what this kit costs, ordered from five different sellers, and waited on shipping three separate times.

The SE 11-Piece Gold Panning Kit exists to prevent that experience. Everything you actually need for a complete first outing — panning, classifying, recovering, and storing your finds — arrives in one box.

## What's in the Box

The kit ships with two black plastic pans (a larger 15-inch and a smaller classifier-style pan), three classifiers at different mesh sizes, a pair of tweezers for picking up color, a snuffer bottle for drawing up fine gold without losing it, a sand magnet for pulling black sand out of your concentrate, and glass vials with rubber stoppers for storing your finds properly.

That list covers every step of the field process. You screen your raw material through the classifiers, work the concentrate down in the pan, use the snuffer to pull up fine gold you can see but can't grab, tweeze out any flakes worth picking individually, and store everything in the glass vials. The magnet helps remove black sand (magnetite) from your concentrate before final panning, which cleans up the gold nicely.

It's a complete workflow, not just a collection of stuff that fits in a box. Whoever spec'd this kit understood how field gold recovery actually works.

## The Pans

The two included pans are functional black plastic pans with riffle systems. They'll get you through a solid day of panning. The 15-inch pan handles your initial work — taking the classified material and panning it down — while the smaller pan works well for final cleanup passes.

What the pans are not is exceptional. If you pan seriously for a year and develop real technique, you'll likely want to upgrade to a dedicated pan — the Garrett Gravity Trap or a quality Keene pan — where the riffle geometry is more refined and the build quality tighter. But for your first season? These pans will teach you everything you need to know, and they'll do it without costing you $35 on a single pan before you've confirmed the hobby sticks.

The riffles aren't as aggressive as a Garrett pan, but that's not entirely a disadvantage for a beginner. Aggressive riffles also require some technique to work properly — you have to move water at the right angle and speed to activate them. Gentler riffles are more forgiving while you're developing that feel.

## Classifiers and Mesh Sizes

The three included classifiers hit a useful range without going to extremes. You're covered for coarse-to-medium classification, which is the right tool for most placer gold scenarios a beginner will encounter on a first prospecting outing.

If you get serious and find yourself on a fine-gold creek where the color is tiny — what prospectors call flour gold — you'll eventually want finer mesh options. That's when you start looking at a dedicated classifier set. But for the first year of prospecting, the three included meshes handle 90% of the material you're likely to work.

## The Small Tools That Matter More Than You Think

The tweezers, snuffer bottle, and glass vials are the items that separate this kit from a basic pan-and-classifier combo — and they're also the items most beginners undervalue until they've been in the field without them.

Tweezers let you pick up individual flakes and nuggets from your pan cleanly. Without them, you're wet-fingering color out of water, dropping half of it, and getting frustrated. The included tweezers are basic but functional.

The snuffer bottle is critical for fine gold. When you've panned down to the point where you can see gold color but it's too fine to pick individually, you fill the snuffer with water, position the tip near the gold, and release the bulb — the suction draws the gold up into the bottle. It's the right tool for the job, and not having one means fine gold sits in your pan and gets washed out because you can't pick it up any other way.

The glass vials with rubber stoppers are the correct way to store your finds. Zip-lock bags and pill bottles work in a pinch but gold that's been through water and fine sediment tends to stick to plastic. Glass is easier to see into, easier to photograph, and the rubber stoppers actually seal. The included vials are small — appropriate for a first outing's worth of color, not a week-long expedition.
