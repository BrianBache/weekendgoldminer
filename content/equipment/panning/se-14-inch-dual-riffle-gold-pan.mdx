## What Makes a Gold Pan a Gold Pan

A gold pan is not complicated. It's a bowl with a textured bottom designed to help gravity and water movement do what they naturally do with dense material: sort it. Gold, at 19.3 grams per cubic centimeter, is significantly heavier than the quartz, feldspar, and black sand it travels with. Given the right water movement, gold settles while lighter material washes over the lip. The pan's job is to make that sorting more controlled and efficient.

The riffles — ridges molded into the pan surface — create low-pressure zones where gold can drop and be captured while water carries lighter material away. More riffles, more zones. Better-designed riffles, more gold recovered at the same skill level.

The SE 14-Inch Dual Riffle Gold Pan is built around exactly this logic, and at $14.99, it's the entry point that most prospectors in the community recommend to anyone just starting out.

## The Dual Riffle System

Single-riffle pans create one sorting zone — an outer ring of ridges that traps material as you work water over the edge. This works, but it creates a single consolidation point. Everything either makes it past the riffles and out of the pan, or it stays behind the same ridges.

The dual riffle design adds a second set of riffles on an inner slope, creating two distinct trap zones. Larger flakes and small nuggets tend to settle in the outer zone. Finer gold — medium flour gold and fine flakes — gets a second chance to settle in the inner zone before it reaches the lip of the pan. Two trap zones means more recovery at the same technique level, and that matters when you're still developing your panning form.

## The Black Background Advantage

If you're new to prospecting, you might wonder why almost all serious gold pans are black. The answer is simple: gold is yellow, and yellow shows up clearly against black. A tan or green pan makes it genuinely harder to spot fine color — especially flour gold that looks like a faint smear of yellow dust rather than an obvious flake. Black gives you maximum contrast.

The SE pan is black injection-molded polypropylene. It holds the color through years of use without fading significantly. The surface is textured enough to aid gold retention without being so rough it traps material you're trying to wash out during panning.

## Pan Size: Why 14 Inches

Gold pans range from 10 inches to 18 inches or larger. The smaller end is for final cleanup — working down a small amount of concentrate to isolated color. The larger end is for processing volume — taking a full classifier's worth of material and moving through it efficiently.

At 14 inches, this pan hits the practical middle. It handles a solid volume — half a classified bucket, roughly — without being so large it becomes unwieldy in a small creek or hard to control for a beginner. It's also small enough to use for final cleanup work, which means you can carry one pan for both roles rather than needing a large pan and a small cleanup pan.

Most instructional prospecting content uses a 14- or 15-inch pan as the reference point, so technique guides, video tutorials, and advice from experienced prospectors will translate directly to this size.

## Honest Assessment: Where This Pan Has Limits

At $14.99, you're getting functional equipment, not exceptional equipment. The riffle geometry does its job, but it's not as refined as a Garrett Gravity Trap or a Keene Engineering pan where decades of design iteration have gone into optimizing exactly how the riffle angles interact with water flow at different pan speeds.

What that means practically: this pan is forgiving if your technique is rough, which is exactly right for a beginner. As your panning form improves, you'll reach a point where better riffle geometry rewards the precision you've developed. That's the natural upgrade moment — when you've outgrown the pan rather than the other way around.

The build is also lighter than a Garrett pan. For most day trips this is a non-issue, but if you're rough on your gear or plan to use the pan as a general-purpose field tool (some prospectors use their pan for everything from washing specimens to scooping water), a heavier pan handles abuse better.

There's also no ergonomic grip molded into the rim. Premium pans typically include a thumb notch or lip grip on one or two sides that makes extended panning less fatiguing. This pan is a flat edge all around. After a full day of panning — fifty or more pans — your grip and wrist will feel it.

**This SE 14-Inch Dual Riffle (~$14.99):** The smart first purchase. The dual riffle system is a genuine upgrade over single-riffle at minimal additional cost, and the SE brand has the validation history to trust. The $25–30 premium is worth it once you're serious.
