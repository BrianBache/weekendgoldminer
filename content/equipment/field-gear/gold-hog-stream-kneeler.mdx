## Your Knees Don't Know It's a Hobby

Talk to anyone who's spent a full day sniping creek bedrock — pressing their knee into angular gravel, leaning forward over a submerged crack, working material out of a horizontal crevice with a sniper tool — and the subject of knee pain comes up fast. Not because prospecting is extreme sport, but because hours of sustained kneeling pressure on irregular rock surfaces in cold water takes a specific, cumulative toll that you don't feel acutely until you're driving home.

The easy answer is "just stand up more." The practical problem with that answer is that the gold doesn't move to accommodate your knees. Productive crevices are where they are. Bedrock shelves worth working are at the depth they are. If the best color is in a crack that requires you to kneel for 45 uninterrupted minutes to fully work it out, you kneel for 45 minutes. The question is how well-protected you are when you do.

Most prospectors know about garden kneelers and foam pads. Some carry them to the creek. Most stop after the first time, because a foam pad in a cold creek is one of the least useful pieces of gear you can bring: it absorbs water immediately, turns cold and heavy, compresses flat on the first real rock contact, and floats away the moment you stand up. It solves the problem it was designed for (kneeling in a garden) and fails completely at the problem you need solved (kneeling on irregular submerged rock all day).

Gold Hog noticed this gap and built the product that actually fills it.

## What Makes This Different

The Gold Hog Stream Kneeler is constructed with two materials chosen specifically for in-water use. The core is high-density EVA foam — the same material used in quality wading boots and whitewater paddling equipment. EVA foam has a critical property that standard open-cell foam lacks: it's a closed-cell structure that doesn't absorb water. Push it underwater for an hour and it comes out exactly as buoyant and cushioning as when it went in. The density is high enough that it doesn't compress flat under sustained weight on a gravel surface — it maintains meaningful loft across hours of use.

The outer cover is neoprene — the same material used in wetsuits. Neoprene is waterproof, insulating, and abrasion-resistant. It repels water off the outer surface rather than soaking it in. The neoprene face that presses against the creek bottom is smooth enough to slide across rock without hanging up, yet textured enough to resist the kind of shifting that flat foam creates under movement.

The combination creates a pad that performs the same wet as it does dry. After an hour in a cold creek on hard bedrock, you still have cushioning. After a full day, your knee still has protection. That's not what generic foam delivers.

## The Strap System

The strap attachment is what separates the Stream Kneeler from every "put this under your knee" solution a prospector has tried before. Two adjustable buckle straps wrap around your leg and secure the kneeler in place. The straps are designed to fit over waders — the geometry accounts for the added bulk of a wader leg — and they tighten enough to hold the pad firmly without creating circulation restriction.

When you stand up to reposition, the kneeler comes with you rather than floating downstream. When you kneel back down, it's already positioned. This sounds like a minor convenience, but on a creek day where you're shifting position constantly — standing to check a classifier, kneeling to snipe a crevice, standing to reposition, kneeling again — the difference between a pad that stays in place and one you have to chase and reposition every cycle is significant. After 50 cycles of stand-repositon-kneel in a full day, it's the difference between a tool that works and a tool you eventually leave on the bank.

## The Sniping Context

Sniping is the prospecting technique that makes the Stream Kneeler most valuable. If you're not familiar with the term: sniping is the practice of targeting bedrock crevices, potholes, and depressions where gold settles and accumulates over time. Gold, being 8 times heavier than the average stream gravel, works its way down through loose material until it reaches hard bedrock. If there's a crack or crevice in that bedrock running perpendicular to the current, gold will collect in it. Heavy accumulations in a single crack can be remarkable — old-timers called these "pay streaks" when they ran along the bedrock contact.

Working a productive crevice requires getting close. You're face-down or sitting back on your heels with your hands inside the crack, using a pick or sniper tool to work material out, then sucking the loosened material into a suction bottle or aspirator. The creek bottom is exactly where your knees are during all of this. The more productive the crack, the longer you stay. A really good crack — one that pays out in multiple workings — might keep you in that position for the better part of an afternoon.

Without knee protection, productive sniping sessions are limited by pain tolerance rather than gold availability. With the Stream Kneeler, the limiting factor becomes the gold content of the crack. That's the correct limiting factor.

## Maintenance Notes

Rinse the Stream Kneeler in fresh water after every creek day. Neoprene that collects fine sediment, algae, and creek minerals without rinsing will develop odor over time and the outer cover can eventually degrade faster. A 30-second rinse under a spigot or in a bucket is sufficient — you don't need to scrub or soap it. Allow it to dry in a shaded location; prolonged UV exposure accelerates neoprene degradation, just as it does with wetsuits.

Store it away from direct sunlight and petroleum-based products. Neoprene and petroleum don't get along — keep your Stream Kneeler away from gas cans, lubricant sprays, and similar gear in your truck bed.
