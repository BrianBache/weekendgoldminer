## What Actually Gets Destroyed in a Creek Day

You've thought through your waders, your gloves, your boots. You've planned for cold water. What you might not have thought through is everything you're carrying above the waterline that needs to stay dry: your smartphone, which you're using for GPS, photos of strike specimens, and the coordinates of that productive bedrock shelf you found last month. Your permit documents, which you'd rather not have to reprint. Your snacks, because a lunch of wet crackers is a miserable experience. Sample vials for the concentrates you're taking home to pan at the kitchen sink. Batteries for your headlamp. A paper map as a GPS backup.

None of that gear is rated for stream exposure. A single unexpected slip on wet rock — one wet knee in a moving current — can introduce enough water to ruin a smartphone or dissolve the permit that keeps you on the right side of a BLM inspection. The solution is a dry bag. The question is which one.

## Why Weight Matters More Than You Think

The invisible tax of a heavy dry bag compounds throughout a prospecting day. You're already carrying waders or hip waders, boots, a classifier set, a gold pan, potentially a sluice box or suction nozzle, food, and water. Adding a heavy waterproof bag to that load feels minor at the trailhead and increasingly significant at hour four on a remote creek. Every ounce you can eliminate from gear that isn't core to the prospecting work itself is an ounce you've recovered from fatigue.

The Sea to Summit Ultra-Sil Nano in 20L capacity weighs 52 grams. To put that in physical terms: it weighs less than two AA batteries. It packs down to the size of a tennis ball and slips into a side pocket you won't even notice. When you arrive at the creek, unfold it, load it, roll the top three times, clip the buckle, and it's done. The entire mass penalty for having reliable waterproof protection for your electronics is imperceptible in your pack.

This is the argument for paying a premium over cheap PVC bags: a 20L PVC dry bag weighs 200–300g and takes up meaningful pack space even when empty. The Sea to Summit charges $15–$20 more than a basic PVC option but costs you zero weight and zero volume when you're not using it. For a piece of kit you carry on every trip but might only actually need on a handful of them, that packability is real value.

## The Construction Argument

The Ultra-Sil Nano uses 30D ripstop nylon with a silicone coating on both interior and exterior faces — what Sea to Summit designates their Si/Si laminate. The silicone coating does two things: it makes the nylon waterproof, and it dramatically improves the material's UV and abrasion resistance compared to uncoated nylon or PVC.

PVC dry bags work on a different principle. They're essentially vinyl — thick, flexible, and inherently waterproof without any coating. The problem with PVC in field use is brittleness under UV exposure: a PVC bag that spends a season getting sun in the back of your truck or strapped to the outside of your pack will develop surface cracking that compromises waterproofing and eventually causes the material to fail at fold points. The silicone-coated ripstop doesn't have this failure mode. It stays flexible and the waterproofing remains intact across years of UV exposure.

The ripstop pattern — small reinforcing fibers woven in a grid through the nylon — stops any puncture from propagating. If something sharp creates a small hole, the ripstop grid contains the damage at that point rather than allowing it to tear into a larger failure. For the kind of gear you're packing alongside metal classifier frames, sluice box hardware, and sharp rock samples, that puncture resistance matters.

## The Roll-Top System

The closure is a roll-top rather than a zipper or buckle seal. This is the right choice for field use: roll-top closures work with wet, muddy, cold-numbed hands in a way that zipper seals don't. Zippers need clean tracks, careful alignment, and fine motor control to operate correctly — everything that cold hands compromise. A roll-top just needs you to roll it down three times and clip the buckle, and it's done.

The three-roll minimum matters. Prospectors who roll once or twice and call it sealed are leaving a gap at the top of the bag that becomes a vulnerability in any meaningful wave or splash. Roll three complete turns — you'll see the top flatten and the sides compress — then clip the buckle. That's the seal. Consistently doing it correctly makes it routine after the first few uses.

The 20L size benefits from the roll-top's adjustability: if you're carrying a small load, you can roll more turns and reduce the dead space inside the bag; if you're stuffing it full, you have room to get your full roll closure even when the bag is at capacity.

## Size Selection for Prospectors

Seven sizes from 1L to 35L means you can right-size this purchase rather than compromising. For reference:

**1L–4L:** Sample vials, phone, emergency cash, permit cards. Fits in a chest pocket.

**8L:** Phone, snacks, documents, extra batteries, small first aid. Day prospecting essentials in one bag.

**20L:** Everything in the 8L plus a spare base layer, lunch, camera, GPS unit, extra gloves. Full day kit.

**35L:** Overnight camp essentials, camera system, laptop. Base camp waterproof storage.

For a prospecting day trip, the 20L is the recommended sweet spot: large enough to hold everything fragile in one organized bag, small enough to fit in a large pack side pocket or clip to a belt loop on your waders. Carrying a 35L when a 20L does the job adds volume and a few grams you don't need.

If you're sampling a new creek and traveling light with just a pan and classifier, consider the 8L. It handles your phone, permit, and snacks in a package you can tuck into a hip belt pocket.

## The Electronics Case Specifically

Your smartphone is the highest-value item that faces the most risk on a creek day. It's your GPS, your camera for documenting strike specimens and recording the exact location of productive bedrock, and your research tool when you're sitting in the truck at the end of the day wondering about the geology you just worked. Replacing one is $500–$1,200 depending on what you carry.

Most smartphones are now water-resistant — IP68 ratings appear on many flagship models. But IP68 tests are conducted in still fresh water under controlled conditions; cold, turbid, fast-moving creek water with mineral content is a different environment. The resistance degrades over time as gaskets age. And IP68 doesn't cover salt spray, mud infiltration, or the sustained moisture exposure of a full day in a wet environment.

Put the phone in the dry bag. This is not overthinking it. The bag weighs nothing, the phone costs hundreds, and the marginal probability of creek exposure destroying a phone you didn't protect is real enough to make the decision automatic.

## How It Fits Into a Prospecting Kit

The practical workflow on a creek day: load the Ultra-Sil Nano at the trailhead before you suit up. Phone goes in (screen capture the permit beforehand if it's in a digital app). Permit folded in a pocket inside the bag. Sample vials loose at the bottom. Snacks in a zip-lock inside the bag — not because zip-locks are wet, but because food smells can attract curious wildlife and a dry bag within a dry bag is good practice in bear country. Extra batteries wrapped in a sock to keep them from puncturing the bag lining. Roll three times, clip, clip to your pack or slip into a side pocket.

That's 90 seconds at the trailhead. That's the cost of not losing gear to a wet day.
