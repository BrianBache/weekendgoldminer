## The Step That Actually Gets Gold in the Vial

Walk through the full cleanup chain that serious miners use. A magnetic wand pulls the magnetite out of your concentrates. A Blue Bowl or Gold Cube separates gold from non-magnetic black sand. A spiral wheel or shaker table may take another pass at the remaining heavies. By the time you're done, you have a small amount of dense material in a collection bowl — significantly upgraded from what you started with, but not clean gold yet.

This is where most miners switch tools. Not to another concentrator or piece of powered equipment. To a small pan.

The finishing pan is the last step in concentrate cleanup. It's the tool that gets you from "upgraded concentrates" to "clean gold in the vial." And the reason you use a small pan — 8 to 10 inches in diameter — instead of your regular 14-inch sluice pan is simple: at this stage of cleanup, you're working tiny amounts of material. Precision matters more than capacity. A small pan gives you the control to work 3 tablespoons of concentrates without accidentally sending flour gold over the edge.

## Why Size Matters for Final Cleanup

A 14-inch gold pan is optimized for processing volumes. You want to move half a bucket's worth of classified material in a reasonable amount of time, and the large pan size gives you the capacity to do that. The trade-off is that working very small amounts of material in a large pan is difficult — the material rattles around, you can't control the water dynamics precisely, and fine gold has a longer path to the lip and more opportunities to escape during the panning motion.

An 8 or 10-inch finishing pan inverts that trade-off. The small diameter means you're always working a controlled amount of material in a controlled depth of water. The pan is light enough to hold easily in one hand with good wrist control. The shallow depth means color is always visible against the black background. When there are only 50 particles of gold in the pan, you want to see every one of them.

The SE finishing pan set typically includes one 8-inch and one 10-inch pan — occasionally with a third 6-inch pan added. The 10-inch is your workhorse for moderate amounts of cleaned-up concentrates. The 8-inch is for the true final-final cleanup: the last tablespoon of heavy material where you're counting individual flakes.

## Material and Visibility

Black polypropylene is the standard material for finishing pans, and for good reason: gold shows up dramatically against a black background. Even flour gold — the barely-visible particles that can hide against lighter backgrounds — stands out as a bright yellow or orange-yellow speck against matte black plastic.

The SE finish is slightly textured, which helps material cling during the early panning stages and gives you feedback through the pan's movement. Some sets include shallow riffles molded into the bowl; others are smooth. Riffles help trap material during the first few passes and give beginners a more forgiving experience. Smooth bowls require you to control the water dynamics through technique alone — it's a slightly higher skill bar, but experienced panners prefer smooth for the final step because it lets them move material exactly where they want it.

When you buy, check whether the set includes riffles. If it does and you're an experienced panner, you may prefer a smooth-bowl option. If you're still building your technique, riffled finishing pans give you a more forgiving experience during the final cleanup stage.

## How to Use a Finishing Pan Effectively

By the time material reaches the finishing pan, most of the waste has been removed. You're working dense heavies: fine gold, some remaining black sand, possibly garnet and other dense minerals. The panning technique shifts accordingly.

Fill the pan about halfway with clear water. Add your concentrate — no more than 2 to 4 tablespoons for a first pass. Submerge the pan, level it, and use a gentle circular or side-to-side motion to stratify the material. Gold sinks fastest; lighter material rises. Work the pan in a shallow submersion, tilting slightly toward you so that water and suspended light material wash gently over the near edge while heavier material stays behind.

The key at this stage is patience and attention. You're not moving bulk; you're isolating individual particles. A consistent, gentle motion beats rushed aggressive panning. If you're losing color, you're working too hard — slow down and let gravity do its job.

When you're down to a small amount of dense material visible against the pan bottom, use a snuffer bottle or a fine dropper to pick up individual gold particles and transfer them to your vial. Some miners do a quick gold swirl to concentrate everything into one spot before picking up.

## Where the Finishing Pan Sits in the Cleanup Chain

The finishing pan is the endpoint, not a standalone solution. Understanding where it fits:

**After a magnetic wand:** You've reduced concentrate volume. Run the remaining material through a Blue Bowl or similar concentrator. The output from that goes into the finishing pan.

**After a Blue Bowl:** The center depression material from a Blue Bowl run still contains non-magnetic black sand alongside gold. This goes directly into the finishing pan for final separation.

**After a Gold Cube:** The vortex mat rinse from a Gold Cube run is the natural final-pan candidate. The mats are rinsed into a small container; that material goes into the finishing pan.

**After a spiral wheel or shaker table:** The collection zone output from these tools still requires a final hand-pan to pick up the gold cleanly.

At every stage, the finishing pan is what closes the loop. No cleanup chain is complete without one.

## Set vs. Individual Pan: What to Buy

The finishing pan set (8" + 10" together) typically runs $10 to $25 on Amazon under various brand names including SE. Buying as a set is almost always the better value — the price difference between a set and a single pan is usually $3 to $8, and having both sizes gives you flexibility.

Avoid sets that don't specify black polypropylene — clear or natural-colored plastic makes visibility poor. Check the listing photo; if the pan is black inside and out, you're in the right territory. If it's some other color or clear plastic, look for a different listing.

At $15 for a typical set, this is the cheapest piece of purposeful equipment you'll buy in gold mining. There's no argument for spending more unless you're looking at a specific specialty pan for a particular technique. The basic black polypropylene finishing pan is the standard for a reason.

## A Note on Snuffer Bottles

Most cleanup chains end with a snuffer bottle — a small squeeze bottle with a narrow tip used to suck up gold particles and transfer them to a vial. If your finishing pan set doesn't include one, add a snuffer bottle to your order. They cost $3 to $8 and are the right tool for picking up color without using your finger (which can cause flour gold to float on skin oils rather than dropping into the vial).

The snuffer bottle is as important to the final step as the finishing pan itself. Buy both. If you already have one, make sure the tip is clean and unclogged before your next cleanup session.

## Compared to Alternatives

**DIY plastic container (~$0):** A small dark plastic container works for the mechanics of final panning, but without polypropylene texture and proper bowl geometry, you're fighting the tool rather than working with it. Fifteen dollars for a real finishing pan is worth it.

**Full-size 14" pan (~$15–$25):** Better for processing larger volumes of pre-classified material but actively harder to use for small final-cleanup amounts. Some miners use a 14" pan throughout and accept the reduced control. Most experienced cleanup miners keep a finishing pan specifically for the final step.

**Gold pan + classifier set (~$100+):** If you don't have a full panning kit yet, a combined classifier and pan set is a better starting purchase than a standalone finishing pan. Get the full panning setup first; add finishing pans as your cleanup chain matures.

The SE finishing pan set is neither dramatic nor complex. It's a small, black, inexpensive plastic pan that gives you precise control over the final step in concentrate cleanup. Every cleanup chain — Blue Bowl, Gold Cube, shaker table, hand pan — ends with something like this. Once your powered or gravity concentrators have done the heavy lifting, the finishing pan is what gets clean gold into your vial.

For $15, it's the easiest purchase decision in your cleanup kit. Buy the set before you need it. You'll need it.
