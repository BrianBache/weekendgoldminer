## When You Want to See the Separation Happen

There's a moment that serious gold miners reach where the existing cleanup tools aren't wrong, exactly — they work, they recover gold — but they feel like black boxes. You pour material in, material comes out, and you trust that the physics are doing what you think they're doing. But you can't see it. You can't watch the gold move, can't adjust on the fly, can't collect the different heavy mineral fractions separately.

The vibrating shaker table changes all of that. It's the one cleanup tool where you stand back and watch the separation occur in real time across the table deck. Gold migrates toward one corner. Lighter black sand travels a different path to a different edge. The distinct fractions are visible as bands of material crossing the oscillating deck. You can see what's working, what's not, and where to adjust.

That visibility is part of why serious miners with significant concentrate volumes eventually end up at a shaker table. It's not just about throughput — though the throughput is substantially better than single-stage concentrators. It's about having complete, observable control over your separation process.

## How a Shaker Table Works

The operating principle of a vibrating shaker table is elegantly simple: a slightly tilted deck oscillates at a controlled frequency and amplitude, while water flows across the surface from a higher edge to a lower one. The deck surface is machined or formed with diagonal riffle grooves — raised ridges running at a slight angle across the table.

Material fed onto the high end of the table is immediately exposed to water flow, vibration, and gravity acting in combination. Dense material — gold, platinum group metals, heavy sulfides — is caught in the riffle grooves and migrates slowly across the table toward the heavy mineral collection edge, carried by the asymmetric oscillation (slow push, fast return) that shaker tables use to move dense particles against the water flow direction. Lighter material — fine sand, silt, lighter black sand — is swept by the water flow off the low edge into the waste collection.

The result is a table that simultaneously separates heavy from light and sorts heavies by density. You don't just get gold versus non-gold; you get a visible spectrum of material from gold (densest, migrates farthest) through mid-range heavies (garnet, magnetite, cassiterite) to light waste. Each fraction can be collected separately at the table's edge, giving you information about your concentrates beyond just the gold content.

## Setup and Calibration: More Complex Than Simpler Tools

A shaker table has more variables than any other hobbyist concentrator. Before you get consistent results, you'll be adjusting:

**Table tilt (deck angle):** Controls how aggressively gravity moves material across the table toward the waste edge. Too steep and material moves too fast, carrying gold into the waste stream. Too flat and material piles up rather than separating.

**Oscillation speed and stroke:** Governs how fast and how far the deck moves per cycle. Higher speed with shorter stroke suits finer material; lower speed with longer stroke suits coarser concentrates.

**Water flow rate:** The volume of water flowing across the deck affects the separation boundary between heavy and light minerals. Increase flow and the boundary shifts; decrease it and you risk fine material piling up.

**Feed rate:** How quickly you introduce material onto the table. Feed too fast and material piles up before it can separate; feed too slowly and you're not using the table's capacity.

These four variables interact. Changing one often means adjusting another. Expect a full session — three to four hours — dedicated entirely to calibration before your first productive run. Take notes. Every variable combination that works for your concentrates is worth writing down.

After calibration, subsequent sessions require minimal adjustment as long as your concentrates are from similar material. Miners who document their settings and return to the same areas report that setup is quick once the initial work is done.

## What You Can Collect from a Shaker Table Run

This is the feature that distinguishes a shaker table from all other cleanup tools: you can collect multiple fractions from the same run.

As the separation bands develop across the table deck, you'll see distinct zones: gold and platinum group metals at the heavy migration edge, magnetite and garnet in a middle band, and fine sand and silt flowing off the light edge continuously. With a small deflector or collection card positioned at the heavy edge, you can collect just the gold-bearing fraction into one container while the middle-range heavies go to a separate one.

Why does this matter? Because experienced miners often want to sort their concentrates for mineralogical reasons beyond gold. Cassiterite (tin ore), platinum group metals, and high-quality garnet have their own value. A shaker table lets you isolate these fractions in a way that no bowl or spiral wheel can replicate.

For the average weekend prospector, this may be more capability than you need. But if you're mining productive ground and want to maximize the value of everything you're recovering, the shaker table's ability to separate distinct mineral fractions is a genuine advantage.

## Throughput and Real-World Use

A hobbyist-grade shaker table processes concentrates faster than a Blue Bowl or Camel Spiral Wheel, but throughput comparisons require honesty: the shaker table is most efficient when you've pre-concentrated your material. Running raw sluice output directly through a shaker table is possible, but you'll get better results and longer uninterrupted runs if you do a quick magnetic separation first and remove the bulk of the magnetic fraction.

Pre-wand your concentrates. Run a first-pass separation through a Blue Bowl or classifier to remove the finest silt. Then feed the shaker table a material that's already partially cleaned. The table's performance on this pre-concentrated feed will be markedly better than running raw concentrates.

In a productive session, a hobbyist-grade shaker table handles multiple buckets of pre-concentrated material — enough cleanup from a full weekend highbanker run to justify the session in one sitting.

## Noise and Space: The Real Considerations

The vibrating motor is audible. Not industrial-loud, but noticeable. In a dedicated outbuilding, workshop, or garage, this isn't an issue. In an apartment or shared living situation, it may be. Plan your workspace accordingly.

The table itself requires a dedicated horizontal surface — a workbench or folding table at a comfortable working height. The table footprint at the hobbyist scale is roughly 12"×24" to 18"×36" depending on the model, plus space for the water collection tub below and the feed system above. This is not a tabletop tool you pack away in a cabinet.

If space is limited, the Blue Bowl or Gold Cube 3-Stack are more practical options. The shaker table rewards miners who have a dedicated cleanup space and process enough volume to justify it.

## Who Should Buy a Shaker Table

A shaker table earns its place if you:
- Process multiple buckets of concentrates per outing and have confirmed productive areas
- Want visual, controllable separation rather than a black-box process
- Have a dedicated workspace where noise and a large footprint aren't constraints
- Are interested in separating distinct mineral fractions beyond just gold
- Have already maximized what simpler tools can do and want the next level

It's not the right starting point. It's where you arrive after you've established that you need it.
