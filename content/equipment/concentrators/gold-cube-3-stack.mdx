## When a Single Pass Isn't Enough

Most concentrate cleanup tools give you one shot at separation. Run your material through a Blue Bowl, collect the heavy fraction, pan the rest out by hand. One stage. Clean enough for casual mining? Sure. But clean enough to capture every flake of flour gold that survived your sluice, your classifier, and the two-hour drive home? That's a harder question.

The Gold Cube 3-Stack was designed for miners who weren't satisfied with "clean enough." It runs your concentrates through three consecutive vortex mat separation stages, each one recovering heavy minerals that the previous stage may have carried past. The result is a cleaner cleanup — one that consistently finds gold in material you've already worked once.

This is a post-field tool in the truest sense. You bring concentrates home from the creek, set up the Gold Cube on your workbench or outdoors station, and run everything through. The three-stage system does in one pass what you'd otherwise need to repeat three times with a single-stage concentrator.

## Understanding the Vortex Mat Technology

The heart of the Gold Cube is its vortex mat — a proprietary texturized surface inside each tray that creates localized turbulence in the water flow. That turbulence mimics the hydraulic conditions of a natural riffle: lighter material is kept in suspension while heavier material drops into the mat's recesses and stays there.

Stack three of these trays vertically and feed water through from below, and material cascades downward from one tray to the next. Gold and heavy minerals that escape the first tray's vortex mat are captured by the second. Anything that escapes the second is caught by the third. By the time material exits the bottom of the stack, the light fraction has been thoroughly separated from the heavy fraction across three independent capture points.

The system runs on a submersible pump — similar to what you'd use for the Blue Bowl — recirculating water from a collection reservoir beneath the stack. The Gold Cube's footprint when assembled is modest: approximately 12 inches square and 18 inches tall, weighing about 8 pounds. That's compact enough to set up on a folding table and genuinely field-portable for base camp use.

Ask any experienced miner what they want out of a cleanup concentrator and "flour gold recovery" is at the top of the list. Coarse flake gold isn't hard to recover — hand-panning gets it fine. Flour gold, the wispy particles barely visible to the naked eye, is where cleanup tools separate themselves.

The Gold Cube 3-Stack has earned its community reputation specifically on fine gold recovery. Miners who run concentrates through a Blue Bowl or spiral wheel, collect what comes out, and then run that "clean" material through a Gold Cube consistently find additional gold. This isn't marketing. It's physics: each additional separation stage catches the material that the previous stage's flow dynamics pushed past the mat. For flour gold that behaves more like suspended sediment than a sinking particle, multiple separation passes make a measurable difference in recovery.

## Setup, Workflow, and Realistic Time Commitment

The Gold Cube takes longer to set up than a Blue Bowl or snuffer bottle. You're assembling three stacked trays, positioning the pump in a water reservoir, running the feed line, and checking your flow rate. Expect 10–15 minutes for first-time setup; experienced users get it down to five.

A typical cleanup session with the Gold Cube runs like this: fill the reservoir with clean water, assemble the stack, start the pump and let flow stabilize, then feed concentrates slowly into the top tray. Work through all your material at a steady pace. When you're done feeding, run clean water through for a few minutes to flush any remaining gold down to the mat collection points. Disassemble in reverse order, rinse the mats into a pan, and do a final hand-pan of whatever the mats produced.

That final hand-pan step — cleaning the vortex mats — is where your gold accumulates. The mats hold the heavy fraction during operation; you recover it at the end. Some miners run the mat rinse through a second Gold Cube pass before hand-panning, squeezing additional recovery from the concentrates.

Total session time for a quart of concentrates: 30–45 minutes including setup and teardown. For two buckets of concentrates from a full weekend highbanker run: a couple of hours. If that time investment aligns with the volume you're processing, the Gold Cube earns every hour.
