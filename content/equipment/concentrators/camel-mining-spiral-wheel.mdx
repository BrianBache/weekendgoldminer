## The Concentrates Are Piling Up

You ran your sluice all Saturday. Sunday too. You're back home with two quart jars of black sand concentrate and the vague sense that there's real gold in there — but panning through it by hand means sitting at a tub for two hours, moving your wrists in the same circular motion until your forearms give out. By the third jar you're sloppy, and sloppy means gold going down the drain.

This is the problem the Camel Mining Spiral Wheel was designed to solve. It's a post-field cleanup tool — something you run at home (or at camp with a DC power source) after the field work is done. The wheel does the hand-panning motion for you, consistently and without fatigue, while you focus on feeding material and watching the results.

## How the Spiral Wheel Works

The concentrator's core mechanism is a shallow dish or flat platter — roughly 14 to 16 inches in diameter depending on the model — that rotates slowly on a motorized base. The surface of the wheel is divided into spiral channels or raised sections that guide material as it rotates. Water is fed onto the wheel from above or through an integrated port, flowing across the spinning surface.

As the wheel turns, denser material — gold, platinum group metals, heavy minerals — migrates toward the center collection zone under the combined influence of centrifugal force, water flow, and gravity. Lighter material — fine sand, silt, lighter black sand — is carried outward and off the edge of the wheel into a collection pan or recirculating system.

The physics are similar to hand-panning: you're using the density difference between gold (specific gravity ~19) and quartz sand (specific gravity ~2.6) to separate them under controlled water flow. The wheel just removes your wrist from the equation.

## Adjustment and Setup

Out of the box, the Camel Spiral Wheel requires some initial tuning to perform at its best. Two controls govern the separation: wheel rotation speed and water flow rate. These interact — faster rotation with too much water flow washes material off too aggressively; too little water and the fine material doesn't separate cleanly.

Most miners find their settings through experimentation. Feed a small test batch of concentrates you've already hand-panned — so you know approximately what gold is present — and adjust until you're seeing the gold migrate toward the center reliably without washing off the edge. Once you have your settings for a given material type, write them down. You'll want them next time.

The 12V DC models give you a field option that the Blue Bowl can't match without a generator. A 12V battery or a reasonably sized power bank can run the spiral wheel at a remote camp, processing your concentrates the same evening you pulled them. That's a genuine convenience if you're doing multi-day trips and want to get ahead of your cleanup before the drive home.

## Performance Across Gold Sizes

The spiral wheel performs reliably on flake gold and coarse fine gold — the kind of material you typically recover from a sluice box or high banker cleanup. For this material, the motorized spiral is very effective and noticeably faster than hand-panning the same volume.

For extremely fine flour gold — the wispy, dust-like material from fine-grained placers — the Blue Bowl's upward-flow vortex approach can be gentler and more consistent. The spiral wheel's rotating action, even at slow speeds, can carry the very finest gold particles outward rather than inward if the water flow isn't perfectly calibrated. If your prospecting area produces significant flour gold, budget extra time for dialing in settings and consider running material through the Blue Bowl afterward as a finishing pass.

For most weekend miners working typical creek and river placers, the spiral wheel's performance range covers everything you'll encounter.

## Maintenance — The Real Conversation

Here's the honest part: any tool with a motor requires more attention than a tool without one. The Camel Spiral Wheel's drive mechanism needs to be kept clear of grit and abrasive sand. After every session, rinse the wheel thoroughly, wipe down the motor housing, and make sure no material has worked into the drive shaft area.

This is not a high-maintenance machine — we're talking five minutes of post-session cleanup. But skip it consistently and you'll eventually shorten the motor's life. The miners who report years of trouble-free operation are also the ones who rinse and dry after every use. The ones who report premature failure usually mention "I left it sitting outside" or "I rinsed it once or twice."

Treat the motor like any 12V DC tool you own. Keep it clean, keep it dry, and it runs indefinitely.

## Throughput and Where It Fits in the Cleanup Chain

The spiral wheel handles 1 to 5 cups per run — roughly comparable to the Blue Bowl in throughput. Where it differs is in operator involvement: the wheel runs independently once you've fed it and started the motor. You can be pulling another batch of concentrates, logging your finds, or doing other campsite tasks while the wheel runs. The Blue Bowl requires similar hands-off time once running, but the spiral wheel integrates more naturally into a multi-task cleanup workflow.

Many miners use the spiral wheel as a first-stage concentrator and follow it with a final hand-pan or snuffer pickup. You run the wheel, collect the center zone material, and then spend five minutes pan-finishing the result. The wheel does 90% of the work; the final pan handles the last 10% with precision.

## Who the Spiral Wheel Is Right For

The Camel Spiral Wheel makes sense if you:
- Regularly come home from the field with more concentrates than you want to hand-pan
- Want a motor-assisted cleanup option without stepping up to the $400 Gold Cube range
- Do multi-day trips and want the option to process concentrates at camp (12V models)
- Understand and accept that a rotating mechanism requires periodic maintenance attention

It's not the right tool if you want zero-maintenance simplicity — that's the Blue Bowl. And it's not the right tool if you're processing high volumes regularly — that's the Gold Cube or a vibrating table.
