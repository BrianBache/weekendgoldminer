## The Upgrade You Should Make Before Your First Run

Most beginner sluice boxes ship without a liner. The SE GP2-52, the Proline 30" Gold Trap, and many other common sluices include only the aluminum frame and riffle hardware. They'll run water and catch heavy material, but the bottom surface of a bare aluminum tray is not a fine-gold retention system — it's a flat metal ramp that gold slides along until it finds a riffle to stop behind, and if the gold is fine enough, it doesn't stop at all. It just rides the current off the tail.

Angus MacKirk ribbed rubber matting is the fix. It goes under your riffles, lining the tray between riffle bars, and turns the empty spaces where fine gold was escaping into a physical capture surface. The ribbed texture creates micro-valleys that gold settles into. The rubber's surface friction holds particles that would slide off flat material. The closed-cell construction doesn't compress under the weight of riffles and gravel — it stays dimensional, so the valleys stay functional.

If you have a sluice box and no matting, or you're running generic carpet or indoor-outdoor carpet from a hardware store, this review is for you. The MacKirk matting is the change you make before you're willing to trust your sluice to recover fine gold.

## Why Matting Material Matters More Than Most Prospectors Realize

The physics of fine-gold capture in a sluice box involve two separate mechanisms. The first is hydraulic: riffle bars create low-pressure zones where reduced water velocity allows heavy material to drop out of suspension. This works reasonably well for coarser gold. The second mechanism is contact: gold that has dropped behind a riffle needs to stay on the collection surface long enough to be covered by more settling material. Contact capture is entirely about the matting — its texture, surface energy, and whether it physically holds particles against the flow that continues to run through the box.

Generic indoor-outdoor carpet has short fiber loops that catch gold physically. The problem is that the loops compress under repeated gravel loading, become coated with fine clay and silt, and lose their trapping ability progressively through a session. By the end of a productive day, generic carpet can be so compacted and coated that it's effectively a smooth surface — and fine gold runs off it.

Angus MacKirk's ribbed rubber doesn't compress. The ribs are formed from closed-cell rubber that retains its shape under load. The valleys between ribs don't fill with silt the way carpet loops do, because the rib walls keep the valley geometry open. When you pull the tray at cleanup, gold that's been collecting in the rib valleys all day is actually there — it hasn't migrated back into the current under the hydraulic load of the next shovel of material.

Ross Boyack's direct comparison video — carpet versus miners moss, with the MacKirk-style ribbed rubber in the mix — shows this difference in measurable terms. It's not a minor refinement. For fine gold recovery, the matting material is as important as the riffle design above it.

## The Hungarian Riffle Combination

Angus MacKirk matting is often sold and discussed alongside Hungarian riffles — angled aluminum strips that run diagonally across the sluice tray rather than perpendicular to flow like standard cross-riffles. The combination of MacKirk ribbed rubber under Hungarian aluminum riffles is the standard DIY upgrade path that turns a budget sluice into a serious fine-gold recovery setup for under $50.

Hungarian riffles work differently from cross-riffles. The diagonal angle creates a lateral sorting action — material doesn't just slow down behind the riffle, it's sorted sideways as it moves through the box. Heavy material consistently migrates toward one side of the tray (the low side), concentrating in a predictable zone. This means your cleanup is more efficient — you know where the gold is going to be.

The MacKirk ribbed rubber under the Hungarian riffles provides the fine-gold contact capture for material that sorts into the riffle zone but needs to be physically held against the current. The aluminum riffles are inert — they sort material, but once something has dropped behind a riffle, the matting below determines whether it stays or washes out. MacKirk rubber keeps it.

You can install this combination in a budget SE sluice for about $45 total — roughly $25 for the matting and $20 for a set of Hungarian riffles cut to your sluice width. The result performs closer to a Keene Engineering setup than to the bare-riffle SE you started with. This is the standard forum recommendation for anyone who doesn't want to spend $185 on a Keene but wants meaningfully better recovery than the entry-level budget box provides.

## Cutting and Installing

Angus MacKirk matting is sold in rolls or by the foot in standard widths: 4", 6", 9", and 12" are the common options. You need to match the width to your sluice's interior tray dimension — not the outside dimension of the box, but the usable interior space between the side walls. Measure your tray width, order the closest available width (or slightly oversized and trim), and cut to length.

The installation is simple: lay the cut piece rubber-side down in the tray, ribbed side up, under your riffle hardware. The matting should fit snugly enough to stay in place when you lift the riffle tray — if it's loose, a small amount of contact adhesive on the tray bottom or a couple of rubber bands around the assembly will keep it positioned during transport.

In the field, cutting to a final fit with scissors takes three minutes. Bring a small pair of scissors in your pack if you're fitting matting at the creek.

## Cleaning the MacKirk Matting

Cleanup with ribbed rubber matting is faster than with carpet. Carpet fibers hold fine gold deep in the fiber bundle, requiring thorough rinsing and sometimes brushing to release everything into your cleanup pan. Rubber ribs release gold more cleanly — a firm rinse with a water bottle or bucket over the rolled-up matting pushes the gold out of the valleys into your pan efficiently.

The standard field cleanup process: pull the riffle tray from the sluice, carry it to your cleanup bucket, hold one end of the tray over the bucket, and roll the matting back toward you while rinsing with clean water. The gold and black sand that's collected in the rib valleys rinses out into the bucket. Unroll, flip, rinse the back face to catch anything that worked through, and you're done. Total time: three to five minutes for a full-length matting section.

After cleanup, the matting is ready to reinstall. Rubber doesn't take on the silt coating that makes used carpet worse over time. Same matting, same performance, session after session.

## Angus MacKirk vs. the Alternatives

**Vs. Generic Indoor-Outdoor Carpet (~$5–10):** This is the comparison most beginners face when they're trying to line a sluice cheaply. Generic carpet catches coarse gold and is better than nothing. But its fibers compress, coat with clay, and degrade faster than rubber. For a one-off trip to test a creek, generic carpet is fine. For your regular kit, MacKirk ribbed rubber is worth the price difference.

**Vs. Miners Moss:** Miners moss is an open-cell polyethylene foam matting used extensively in commercial and serious hobby sluicing. It has excellent fine-gold capture due to its three-dimensional open structure, and many prospectors prefer it over rubber for large-volume operations. The trade-off is cleanup complexity — releasing fine gold from miners moss requires more thorough rinsing and sometimes a chemical soak. For hobbyists, MacKirk rubber is faster to clean and adequate for the grain sizes typically encountered. For serious high-volume operations, miners moss is the professional standard.

**Vs. Miners Moss + Hungarian Riffle Kit (~$70):** This is the premium two-layer combo — miners moss under Hungarian aluminum riffles — that represents the best-available DIY liner system. If you want the highest possible fine-gold recovery from your sluice liner, this combination wins over MacKirk rubber. At $70 for the kit versus $20 for MacKirk alone, the price difference is real but not enormous. Buy the MacKirk setup for most situations; invest in the miners moss + Hungarian riffle combo when you're working a highly productive fine-gold site and you want to maximize every cleanup.
