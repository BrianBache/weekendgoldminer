## The Case for a Packable Sluice

Most prospectors picture a sluice box as a fixed piece of equipment — something you haul to a creek in the back of a truck, wedge into the current, and run material through all day. That's one kind of sluicing, and it works well in accessible locations. But some of the best placer gold in the western United States is sitting in streams that require a two-hour hike to reach. The prospectors working those streams pack everything in on their backs, and every piece of equipment needs to justify its weight.

The Proline 30" Gold Trap Sluice Box is what you bring when the gold is worth hiking to get.

## Where the Packable Sluice Fits in the Workflow

Understanding where any sluice belongs starts with understanding what a sluice actually does. A sluice box intercepts a moving current of water and uses that current to sort material by specific gravity. Gold is roughly 19 times denser than water. As you feed gravel into the head of the box, the current carries the light material — sand, silt, feldspar — downstream and off the tail, while the heavy material — gold, black sand, magnetite — drops to the bottom and gets caught behind the riffles.

A packable sluice like the Proline 30" is your first-stage field separation tool. You're not running finished concentrates through this box — you're processing raw stream material at the dig site. The 30" form factor gives you enough riffle surface for meaningful recovery while still fitting inside or strapping to a pack. Compare that to a full-size 52" aluminum sluice, which you'd run out of a truck at a roadside access point. Or a recirculating system like the Desert Fox, which processes concentrates at camp using a bucket of water. Or a motorized highbanker, which pumps water up to you so you can work away from the main channel. The Proline 30" is your backcountry primary — designed to process gravel in the stream where you found it, then get back in your pack for the hike out.

## What You're Actually Getting

The Proline 30" is a US-made aluminum sluice. The frame is formed from lightweight aluminum — rigid enough to hold its angle in a stream without flexing, light enough to forget it's in your pack. The riffle system uses a removable tray design: the riffles, matting, and underlying tray all come out as one piece, making cleanup genuinely fast. After a session, you pull the tray, carry it to clean water, lift the matting, and rinse your concentrates into a pan. No awkward contortion in the stream. No digging around under fixed riffle bars.

At approximately three pounds, the Proline 30" is competitive with any aluminum packable sluice currently made. You'll feel it less than your lunch after the first hour on the trail.

The 30" length is a considered choice. Go shorter — say 18" to 24" — and you sacrifice riffle real estate in ways that cost you gold. Go longer — 36" to 52" — and you've crossed into territory where the sluice starts fighting you on the trail: harder to balance, awkward to wedge in a moving stream, difficult to hold stable when the current picks up. Thirty inches is the practical optimum for a solo prospector working a mountain creek.

## Setting It Up Solo

Setup is genuinely fast once you've done it a few times. Find a section of stream with at least 6 to 10 inches of head drop — the water needs to fall that much over the sluice length to generate the flow velocity that actually sorts material. Wedge the tail downstream, angle the box at 5 to 8 degrees (a modest slope, not steep), and secure the upstream end so current runs steadily from head to tail without washing over the sides.

In a fast mountain creek with good gradient, you'll wedge rocks under the tail and sides. In a shallower stream, you might redirect a small diversion. The goal is consistent, smooth flow through the box — not turbulent splash, not a trickle. When the water moving through the sluice looks like glass at a walking pace, you've found your angle.

Then you classify. Dropping raw gravel straight into the Proline 30" will technically work, but you'll be moving significant volume for marginal results. Run your material through a 1/8" or 1/4" classifier first, discard the oversize, and feed only the undersize into the head of the sluice. You'll move less volume per shovel, concentrate your recovery on the grain sizes where placer gold actually lives, and pull up your tray at the end of the day with concentrates worth panning down.

## The Matting Gap

The Proline 30" ships as the aluminum frame and riffle tray. Matting is not included. Before your first run, you need to cut and fit a carpet or miners moss liner to the tray. Most experienced prospectors pair this sluice with Angus MacKirk ribbed rubber matting cut to width — it's a $15 to $20 addition that takes fifteen minutes and meaningfully improves fine-gold retention compared to generic carpet.

If this is your first sluice, budget for the matting upfront and cut it before you leave home. Ask for a width that matches the Proline's interior tray dimension — your dealer or a quick message to Proline will give you the exact spec. Showing up at the creek with matting pre-installed saves you from losing an afternoon to improvised solutions.

## Proline 30" vs. the Competition

**Vs. SE GP2-36 (~$35):** The SE gets you sluicing for less than half the Proline's price, and if you're doing your first trip to a creek you've never prospected, that's the right call. You're buying information about the site, not committing to a sluice setup. If the creek pans out and you're going back regularly, upgrade to the Proline. If it's a dud, you haven't overinvested.

**Vs. Garrett 24" Super Sluice (~$95):** Garrett is 6" shorter and around $20 more. The Garrett's multi-stage riffle system — expanded metal over carpet — is a genuine design advantage over basic cross-riffles, but in a 24" box you give up 6" of riffle length to get it. In most field conditions, the Proline's 30" with quality matting performs comparably to the Garrett. Buy the Garrett if Garrett's brand support and tutorial depth matter to you. Buy the Proline if you want US manufacturing, a slightly more generous recovery bed, and a removable tray that cleans up in thirty seconds.

**Vs. Full-Size 52" Sluice:** If you're driving to a car-accessible creek and not hiking, get a full-size sluice. The 52" gives you dramatically more riffle surface area and throughput. The Proline 30" is specifically a trail tool — it's not trying to be anything else.

## Is the Proline 30" Right for You?

This sluice is built for the prospector who hikes. If your creek is accessible by truck and weight isn't a constraint, you should be running a longer box. But if you've studied topo maps, spotted a fork three miles up a trail that shows classic placer-forming geology — a tight bend, an inside gravel bar, exposed bedrock on the outside — and you want a sluice you can carry in and trust when you arrive, the Proline 30" is it.

It's also the right answer if you want to own one quality packable sluice indefinitely rather than upgrade through a succession of cheap alternatives. The Proline won't need replacing. It'll outlast the boots you hike it in on.

The one thing to know before you buy: you're not ordering from a Prime listing. You'll contact Proline directly or find an authorized dealer. That takes a couple more steps than most Amazon purchases. The tradeoff is a sluice built by people who understand gold mining at a fundamental level, sold by a company that will pick up the phone if something goes wrong.

Buy the SE GP2-36 if you want to test the concept cheaply. Buy the Proline 30" when you're ready to take remote-stream prospecting seriously.
