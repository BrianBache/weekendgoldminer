## The End of the Frequency Scale

Every metal detector manufacturer who builds a VLF gold machine makes a claim about frequency — and with good reason. The relationship between operating frequency and sensitivity to small gold is direct: higher frequency equals better detection of small, low-conductivity targets. This isn't a marketing narrative; it's physics.

Fisher Research Labs pushed this principle further than anyone else in the industry and built the Gold Bug 2. At 71 kHz, it operates at the highest frequency of any commercially manufactured VLF gold detector. Not among the highest. The highest. Nobody has shipped a production VLF gold machine that runs faster.

This is a machine with a genuine technical distinction in a category where most products are competing on features and price. If maximum VLF sensitivity for fine gold is your specific requirement, the Fisher Gold Bug 2 is the only option in this category that delivers it.

## What 71 kHz Actually Means in the Field

The math is straightforward: the Gold Bug 2 operates at nearly four times the frequency of the Garrett AT Gold (18 kHz) and roughly 15% higher than the Nokta Gold Kruzer (61 kHz). In practical terms, this translates to detection of targets that those machines cannot register.

In California and Nevada placer fields — particularly the hydraulic mine tailing grounds and fine-gold creek drainages of the western Sierra Nevada — the gold is often extremely fine. We're talking about flakes that barely register in a gold pan, wire gold that follows quartz seams in micron-thick films, flour gold that colors a pan without producing a visible piece. In these deposits, an 18 or even 61 kHz machine is frequently below detection threshold for the smallest and most abundant fraction of the gold present.

Fisher Metal Detectors' official YouTube channel has field footage of the Gold Bug 2 operating in exactly these conditions — it's not a bench demonstration. The machine finds targets in proven gold ground that other machines passed over on prior hunts. This is verifiable, repeatable, and field-documented.

Experienced prospectors who've worked heavily hunted ground with the Gold Bug 2 report finding overlooked gold in patches that the conventional wisdom said were "hunted out." The machine isn't magic — but at 71 kHz, it's hearing things no previous pass at lower frequencies could have heard.

## The Audio Instrument

There is no digital target ID on the Gold Bug 2. There is no color screen, no audio tone segments, no display showing you a target conductivity estimate. There's an analog meter, a threshold control, a sensitivity adjust, and a boost switch.

This is entirely intentional, and it's either the machine's greatest strength or its primary obstacle depending on who's holding it.

The audio threshold tone is the primary detection instrument on the Gold Bug 2. You set the threshold — a faint background hum that tells you the machine is working — and target responses are heard as variations against that background. A strong target produces a clear audio peak. A marginal target — one at the edge of detection — produces a subtle undulation in the threshold tone, sometimes barely distinguishable from background variation.

Experienced users learn to read these sub-threshold signals. They hear whispers that register as curiosity rather than certainty, and they develop the discipline to investigate those whispers anyway. This is where many of the genuinely significant finds on fine-gold machines come from — not from obvious, clear signals, but from the marginal responses that a trained ear follows to a target.

The boost switch is designed for exactly this situation. When you suspect a faint signal but aren't certain, the boost function increases gain on that target position, amplifying what was marginal into something more clearly audible. It's a confirmation tool more than a scanning feature, but used correctly, it's powerful.

The honest implication: this machine is not appropriate as a first detector. The learning curve on analog audio detection is real and takes months of field time to develop. If you put a beginner with a Gold Bug 2 next to a beginner with a Garrett AT Gold in the same patch, the AT Gold user will almost certainly find more gold in the first session — because the digital ID gives them a framework for decision-making that the Gold Bug 2's audio-only interface doesn't provide.

Put an experienced user behind the Gold Bug 2 in the same scenario, and the equation flips.

## Ground Balance at 71 kHz: Precision Required

The Gold Bug 2 uses the same manual push-button ground balance system as the original Gold Bug — you sweep the coil over representative ground, press to lock, and the machine stores the mineral signature as background. At 71 kHz, this process is more sensitive and more important than at lower frequencies.

Higher frequency means higher sensitivity to everything — including ground minerals. In lightly mineralized ground, this sensitivity is pure asset. In heavily mineralized ground, the same sensitivity that hears micro-gold also amplifies every variation in the mineral matrix, making threshold stability harder to maintain.

The technique for managing this is to ground balance frequently as you move across variable terrain, use the threshold control to set your background tone conservatively (not too hot), and learn to recognize the difference between mineralization-induced threshold variation and an actual target. This is a learnable skill — prospectors who run the Gold Bug 2 regularly in variable ground develop a feel for it that becomes nearly automatic.

In extreme mineralization — the high-iron red soils of Arizona and parts of Nevada — the 71 kHz sensitivity can become a liability. In those conditions, a lower-frequency machine (or a PI machine that ignores mineralization entirely) may actually outperform the Gold Bug 2 by being more stable. The Gold Bug 2's ideal environment is areas with moderate mineralization and fine gold — the classic California Sierra Nevada drainage profile.

## The 6.5" Elliptical Coil and Ground Coverage

The standard configuration ships with a 6.5-inch elliptical coil — slightly larger than the Fisher Gold Bug's 5-inch round DD but still firmly in the precision-hunting size class. The elliptical shape concentrates the detection pattern in a way that's effective for bedrock crack hunting and methodical sweeping of small areas.

If you're working open terrain — wide gravel bars, flat tailings areas, desert washes without heavy brush — Fisher also offers the Gold Bug 2 packaged with a 10-inch elliptical coil, and you can swap coils between configurations. The 10-inch covers ground significantly faster and is better suited to reconnaissance hunting over new areas. Most serious Gold Bug 2 users own both sizes and select based on the day's task.

The coil is waterproof; the control housing is not. This is the same limitation as the Gold Bug 1 and it's the machine's most significant practical constraint for prospectors who work active stream environments. You can swing the coil in shallow water — typical creek-edge hunting is fine — but you keep the control box out of any scenario where it might get submerged. Rain is not a problem; submersion is.

## Who Should Buy the Gold Bug 2

You're a prospector with at least a season of field experience on a lower-frequency machine. You've worked fine-gold ground and you've confirmed — through test panning, sample analysis, or direct comparison — that the gold present is finer than your current machine reliably detects. You have the audio detection skills to work an analog-only machine productively.

Or you're a serious hobbyist who wants the best VLF sensitivity available and is committed to investing the learning time to use it effectively. You're going to dedicate your first five or six outings to developing your audio vocabulary on this machine, and you understand that the payoff comes after that investment, not before.

The Gold Bug 2 is also excellent in quartz vein country as a specimen detector. Visible gold in quartz matrix produces strong signals at moderate depths; the machine's sensitivity catches telltale gold even when it's not visible to the eye. If you're doing specimen hunting in productive hard rock areas, the 71 kHz sensitivity gives you an edge in finding matrix that casual inspection misses.

## Comparing the Fisher Family

Fisher makes three machines worth considering for gold prospecting: the Gold Bug ($349), the Gold Bug Pro ($499), and the Gold Bug 2 ($849).

The Gold Bug is the entry point — 19 kHz, manual ground balance, analog display, 5-inch DD coil. Best for beginners who want a purpose-built gold machine without spending too much.

The Gold Bug Pro adds digital target ID and Iron Audio at 19 kHz for $499 — a meaningfully better user interface at the same frequency. If you want digital ID and the Fisher platform, the Pro is the correct choice over the standard Gold Bug.

The Gold Bug 2 is the specialist's machine — 71 kHz, analog only, built for fine gold. It's not a "better" Gold Bug in the way the Pro is an improvement — it's a different tool for a different specific task. The frequency jump from 19 to 71 kHz is the purchase justification, and if that frequency difference doesn't matter in your specific hunting conditions, you're paying a premium for a disadvantage (more audio management) with no offsetting benefit.

## The Bottom Line

The Fisher Gold Bug 2 at 71 kHz occupies a unique position in the VLF gold detector market — it's the highest-frequency production machine available, and in fine-gold terrain, that distinction is real and field-verifiable. Experienced users working California placer fields and other fine-gold environments consistently report finds that lower-frequency machines cannot produce.

The trade-offs are equally real: no digital target ID, no waterproofing on the control housing, and a demanding audio interface that requires genuine field time to master. This is an expert's tool, priced accordingly, with a specific use case where it's genuinely the best option available.

If you're new to detecting, start with the Gold Bug or the Garrett AT Gold and build your skills. When frequency becomes the limitation — when you know the gold is there and your current machine isn't hearing it — the Gold Bug 2 is the answer.
