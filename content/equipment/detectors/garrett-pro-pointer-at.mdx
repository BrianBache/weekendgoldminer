## The Part of the Dig That Goes Wrong

Your detector gives a clear signal. You dig a plug, set it aside, and check the hole — still a signal. You remove another scoop of material, check again — it's in the loose material now. You sift through it with your hands, pass it under the coil, shuffle it around — and somewhere in the process, the target ends up back in the hole, or mixed into the pile, and you're now hunting through a scattered mess of gravel trying to recover a gold flake that could be anywhere.

This happens. It happens to experienced prospectors. It happens more often when the target is small, when the ground is wet, when the light is bad, when you're tired, and when the gravel is the same color as the thing you're looking for. A pinpointer doesn't prevent this problem entirely — but it closes the gap between "I dug a signal" and "I have the target in my hand" by giving you a focused probe that can locate the target within the loose material with precision.

The Garrett Pro-Pointer AT is the pinpointer that over 2,000 verified buyers put a 4.7-star rating on. That's not a marketing number — that's the accumulated experience of people actually using it in real conditions, on real outings, finding real gold. When a tool earns that kind of consensus across a large and opinionated user base, you pay attention.

## How Pinpointing Actually Works in the Field

Your main detector's coil sweeps a detection field that's typically 6 to 12 inches wide and produces a signal when a target is anywhere within that zone. When you get a signal and dig a hole, you've established that a target exists somewhere in roughly a shovel-width area. That's useful but imprecise — you have cubic inches of material to search.

The Pro-Pointer AT is 8.75 inches long and about 1.4 inches in diameter. It detects from the tip, with the most sensitive zone in the last inch or two. When you push it into loose material or along a hole wall, it responds to targets within a few inches of the tip — giving you a much smaller search zone than your main coil. Sweep the tip through loose material and you'll hear the audio (or feel the vibration) change sharply when you're near the target. Move your hand toward where the signal is strongest, and within a few passes you've narrowed the target to a handful of material you can dump in your palm and inspect.

This process takes seconds when it works well. The alternative — sifting loose gravel with your fingers hoping to see a gold flake — can take minutes and ends with missed finds when the gold is too small to see or drops back into the hole unnoticed.

## Three Sensitivity Levels: Not a Gimmick

The three sensitivity settings on the Pro-Pointer AT exist because gold hunting creates specific detection challenges that require adjustable response.

**Level 3 (maximum sensitivity)** is for open-air use and initial scanning — when you've dumped loose material on a flat rock or into a pan and want maximum detection range to locate the general area of the target.

**Level 2 (medium)** is the working level for most digs. It gives you good sensitivity while keeping false signals from nearby mineralized soil manageable. When you're probing a hole wall in iron-bearing creek gravel, Level 3 may trigger on the soil itself; Level 2 stays quieter until the tip is genuinely close to a metal target.

**Level 1 (minimum)** is for close-in confirmation. Once you've located the general area of the target, switching to minimum sensitivity lets you get the tip extremely close and pinpoint precisely — within a centimeter or two — so you can pick the exact piece out of material rather than picking up a handful.

Garrett's instructional video on YouTube walks through exactly this sensitivity workflow. It's seven minutes and directly translates to better target recovery in the field. Watch it before your first outing with the pinpointer; you'll recoup the learning time on your first successful recovery.

## Waterproofing That Earns Its Keep in Gold Country

The places where gold concentrates are wet places. Bedrock in active creek channels. The inside curves of streams where material settles in pools. Submerged gravel bars that haven't been worked because reaching them requires getting in the water. Crevices in canyon walls where seasonal runoff leaves fine gold in every crack.

A non-waterproof pinpointer is effectively useless in all of these environments. You've found a signal with your waterproof main detector, you've located the target zone, and now you need to probe wet material and potentially submerged bedrock to find the exact target — and your pinpointer can't get wet. This is the scenario that converts skeptics about spending $149 on a pinpointer.

The Pro-Pointer AT is submersible to 10 feet. You can probe submerged bedrock, push it through creek-bottom gravel, and use it to chase a rolling target in the current without hesitation. The tip seal and body seal have been engineered to handle the repeated wet-dry cycling that comes from creek work — not just a single submersion test.

The orange color is also not an accident. In the grey-and-brown environment of creek gravel and decomposed granite, an orange probe is findable when you set it down. When you're working a creek and set the pinpointer on a rock while you dig, you will find it again. A black or grey tool in those conditions gets lost regularly.

## Vibration Mode: A Subtlety That Matters

The Pro-Pointer AT can operate in audio-only mode, vibration-only mode, or combined audio-and-vibration mode. The vibration option is more than a convenience for situations where noise would disturb other people — in gold prospecting, it has a specific practical value.

When you're actively swinging your main detector and building a clear audio picture of a target location, stopping to probe with a beeping pinpointer can disrupt your threshold calibration and your audio focus. If you switch the Pro-Pointer to vibration mode, you can use it during or between main detector sweeps without generating competing audio. You feel the target confirmation rather than hearing it. This lets your ears stay on the main detector's threshold while your hands confirm the target location.

Experienced prospectors who run both tools simultaneously — checking detected targets in real time as they work a patch — find vibration mode makes the two-tool workflow smoother and more continuous.

## The 9V Battery Reality

The Pro-Pointer AT runs on a standard 9V battery. Not rechargeable, not proprietary — the kind of battery you can buy at any gas station or hardware store. This is a deliberate design choice: proprietary batteries that require charging and can die with no replacement available are a field logistics problem. A 9V is universal.

The trade-off is that you're consuming disposable batteries. Under normal use (a few hours of intermittent operation per outing), a 9V lasts several outings. Heavy continuous use will shorten that. Carry a spare in your kit — one 9V battery weighs essentially nothing and the alternative is a dead pinpointer in the field.

Garrett includes a battery-pull tab that makes replacement in the field fast (no tools required), which is a practical detail that shows they actually thought about field use conditions.

## The Lost-Alarm: Worth Mentioning Once

The Pro-Pointer AT has an automatic alarm that triggers if the device remains still for approximately five minutes. The intent is to prevent the very common scenario of setting the pinpointer down in a hole or on a gravel bar and forgetting it when you move on.

Lost pinpointers are a real phenomenon. They're small, they're orange but they're still easy to overlook in brush, and the end of a productive outing is exactly when you're most likely to be tired and inattentive. The alarm isn't foolproof — if the pinpointer is buried in your dig material, the alarm might not be audible — but it's a genuine quality-of-life feature that has prevented losses.

Every prospector running any detector. This is not tier-specific — the pinpointing problem is universal from a $349 Gold Bug to a $5,499 GPX 6000.
