## The Creek Is Telling You Something. Here's How to Listen.

The first time most people go gold prospecting, they pick a spot the same way they'd pick a place to sit at a picnic: whatever looks nice, whatever's close to where they parked, wherever there's a flat area to stand. Then they dig, and they wonder why they're not finding anything.

Experienced prospectors walk up to a creek they've never seen before and know within a few minutes where to start. They're not guessing. They're reading. The creek is showing them exactly where a heavy piece of gold would end up if one were moving through the system, and they've learned how to follow that logic.

This is one of the most useful skills in all of recreational prospecting, and it's almost entirely learnable from principles. After that, it's pattern recognition — and pattern recognition gets faster every time you go out.

---

## The Physics Behind Everything

Before the specific features, it's worth making sure the underlying principle is clear, because it's the same principle in every scenario.

Water moving through a stream carries sediment. The carrying capacity of moving water is roughly proportional to the square of its velocity — so water moving at twice the speed can carry roughly four times the mass of material. When the water slows down, its carrying capacity drops fast, and material falls out of suspension.

Gold has a density of 19.3 g/cm³ — more than seven times the density of ordinary stream gravel (around 2.6 g/cm³). This means gold requires far more current energy to stay in motion than ordinary material does. The moment a current slackens — even a minor reduction in velocity — gold drops while lighter material stays in suspension. Gold doesn't travel a few extra feet like lighter sand. It stops, immediately, where the current changes.

The creek is a machine that sorts material by weight every single time it runs, every flood, every season, for thousands of years. Heavy material concentrates in the slow zones. Light material moves downstream toward the ocean. Your job is to find where the machine deposits its heaviest cargo.

Every technique for reading a stream is really just a technique for asking: where does the current slow down?

## Inside Bends: The Classic Starting Point

If you only ever learn one thing about reading a stream, learn this: gold concentrates on the inside of bends.

When a stream curves around a bend, the water on the outside of the curve moves faster — it has more distance to cover in the same time as the inside water. The difference can be significant. In a tight bend, the outside velocity may be 20–30% higher than the inside velocity. The water on the inside of the curve moves slower. Slower water drops material.

The result is a classic geomorphological pattern: on the outside of a bend, the bank is undercut and eroded — fast-moving water is eating at it. On the inside of the bend, material accumulates — a crescent of gravel and sand that the slower water couldn't keep moving. This crescent is called a **point bar**. It's a natural gold trap.

<img src="/images/guides/stream-bend-gold-zones.png" alt="Stream bend gold accumulation diagram" className="w-full rounded-lg my-6 border border-earth-200" />

Not all of a point bar is equally productive. Experienced prospectors know that the gold doesn't spread uniformly across the gravel bar — it concentrates at the **downstream tail of the bar**, where the current makes its tightest transition between slow inside and fast outside. That's the zone where material is dropping most actively. The upstream end of the bar is often less productive because material there has continued traveling with the current; the downstream end is where things stop.

A practical technique: when you arrive at an inside bend, don't start at the most obvious flat gravel area. Walk to the downstream end of the bar where it tapers out and meets the main channel. Dig your first test pans there. If you're getting black sand and flakes, work upstream along the bar and see where the concentration drops off.

## Downstream of Obstructions: Boulders, Logs, and Bridge Pilings

Anything that interrupts the current creates a zone of slower water on its downstream side. Boulders are the most obvious and most important example.

In front of a boulder — upstream of it — water piles up and accelerates around the sides. The pressure differential between the upstream face and the downstream side creates a circulation zone directly behind the boulder: a **wake eddy**, similar to the calm zone behind a ship. Gold moving downstream enters this eddy, slows, and settles.

Here's the part beginners often miss: the productive zone isn't always directly behind the boulder. It's often **further downstream**, at the point where the two currents that split around the boulder's sides rejoin. That rejoining point — where two flow vectors merge — creates turbulence and then, just downstream of the turbulence, a zone of lower velocity as the current reorganizes. This is sometimes called the **downstream convergence zone**, and it can be more productive than the eddy directly behind the boulder.

<img src="/images/guides/boulder-eddy-zones.png" alt="Boulder eddy and convergence zone diagram" className="w-full rounded-lg my-6 border border-earth-200" />

Work both areas systematically. Put a test pan from the immediate downstream eddy and another from 5–15 feet further downstream where the currents rejoin. The better result tells you where this particular boulder is trapping material most effectively.

Large boulders that have been in place for many seasons are more productive than recently deposited ones, because they've had more seasons of accumulation. If you're in an area that experiences annual flooding, boulders that are too large to move during floods are effectively permanent traps. The same boulders have been catching gold every season for a very long time.

Logs follow the same logic as boulders but are often temporary features — a log that's been in a creek for several years may have accumulated significant material, but logs move in major floods. Bridge pilings, rock walls, and any other human-made structure that interrupts the current creates exactly the same hydraulic pattern.

## Bedrock: The Floor of the Trap

Sand and gravel move. Bedrock doesn't. This distinction is the most important geological feature in any prospecting location.

Gold is heavy enough that it sinks through loose material over time. Given years of water movement and vibration — every spring flood, every high-flow period agitating the streambed — gold works its way down through the gravel and sand until it hits something it can't work through: bedrock.

This means the most concentrated gold in any streambed is often not scattered through the gravel. It's sitting at or in the bedrock surface — in the **crevices, pockets, and irregularities** of the rock itself. The material that accumulates in these bedrock traps is called **crevice gold**, and working bedrock crevices is one of the most reliably productive techniques in the hobby.

When you're working an area and you reach or approach bedrock, everything changes. The material directly above bedrock — the last few inches — is where gold concentrates most. Collect this material carefully. Use a **crevice tool** — a long, angled pick or stiff scraper designed to work into rock crevices — to extract the packed material from every crack and pocket. Even crevices that look small can hold more material than you'd expect. Bedrock often has natural pockets, cross-ribs, and irregular surfaces that trap material very effectively.

Not all bedrock is equally productive. **Schist and other foliated metamorphic rocks** are often excellent because they have natural horizontal layering that creates ledges and pockets. **Granite** is typically smooth and offers fewer natural traps, but even granite has joints and fractures that can accumulate material. **Limestone** can develop pitting through chemical weathering that creates effective traps.

The character of the bedrock surface matters. Rough, irregular bedrock with lots of natural pockets is better than smooth, flat bedrock. Cross-ribs — ridges in the bedrock running perpendicular to the current — are especially good: they function like natural riffle bars, catching material on their downstream faces. When you find exposed bedrock with distinct cross-ribs, take that spot very seriously.

A critical technique: **sniping**. This is the practice of working bedrock crevices specifically, usually in low-water conditions when bedrock is exposed. You're not shoveling material and running it through a pan or sluice in bulk — you're working specific crevices, one at a time, extracting the concentrated material from each. Snipers often carry a collection of specialized tools: thin, flexible spatulas to extract packed material, turkey basters or squeeze bulbs to blow silt out of deep crevices, and small collection containers to hold crevice material before panning. It's precise, patient work, and experienced snipers often report very high per-hour gold recovery compared to bulk processing methods.

## Tributary Confluences: Where Two Streams Meet

Any place where a tributary stream enters the main creek deserves special attention. When two currents merge, the combined flow momentarily loses organization — the vectors interfere with each other, velocity drops, and material that both streams were carrying settles.

The specific pattern depends on the relative sizes and velocities of the two streams. If the tributary enters at an angle, the main current often deflects slightly, creating a pocket of slower water on the downstream side of the confluence. The inside of any bend created by the joining currents follows the standard point bar pattern.

Gold deposited at confluences comes from two source directions instead of one, which can make these spots more productive than a random reach of either stream alone. If the tributary drains different geology than the main stream — different rock types, different historical mining activity upstream — the confluence is where those two mineral populations mix. That's worth investigating.

Tributary confluences are also worth checking even when the tributary is dry — a seasonal stream or wash that only carries water during rain events. During a flood, that dry wash can deliver pulses of material from its entire drainage into the main creek. The gold deposited by these episodic flows concentrates at the confluence just as it would in a perennial stream, and it may sit undisturbed between events.

## Reading the Gravel: Color and Texture as Clues

Not all streambed material looks the same, and the variation tells you something about what the water has been doing.

The most important visual indicator is **black sand** — the heavy iron minerals (primarily magnetite) that concentrate in the same slow-water zones as gold. Where the gravel is noticeably darker than the surrounding material, look more carefully. The black sand is telling you that this is a sorting zone — a place where the current has been dropping its heavy load.

The depth of the black sand layer matters too. A thin smear of black sand in the surface gravel is less significant than a deep, dense layer of black sand extending several inches down. The deeper the concentration, the longer it's been accumulating, and the more seasons of gold deposition it may represent.

**Grain size changes** in the gravel also carry information. Very coarse material — large cobbles and boulders — was deposited during high-energy flow. Very fine material — silty sand — was deposited in very calm water. A transition from coarse to fine, moving from upstream to downstream within a short distance, marks a zone where velocity dropped significantly. That transition zone is often productive.

**Clay layers** are worth examining carefully. A band of clay within the gravel profile — exposed in a bank cut or test hole — represents a former surface where material accumulated over a long period. Gold, which sinks through loose material, can concentrate above a clay layer the same way it concentrates above bedrock, because clay is relatively impermeable. When you encounter clay in a bank, the material immediately above it deserves extra attention.

## How to Sample a New Area Efficiently

When you arrive at a stretch of creek you haven't worked before, systematic sampling is more efficient than committing to any single location without testing. Here's the approach experienced prospectors use.

**First, walk the creek before you dig.** Spend 10–15 minutes walking the stretch you're considering, looking for the features described in this guide: inside bends with point bars, large boulders, exposed bedrock, tributary confluences, areas where the gravel is noticeably darker. Note the features and roughly prioritize them.

**Then run quick test pans from each location.** A test pan doesn't need to be a full, meticulous pan — you're trying to assess whether a location has any gold at all, not to maximize recovery. Fill your pan from the likely spot (ideally from near bedrock or from the base of a point bar), run it down to the last inch or two of material, and look. Black sand is a good sign. A flake is a very good sign. Nothing but light quartz sand is a signal to move on.

**Interpret absence carefully.** No gold in a test pan doesn't mean no gold in the area. It might mean you sampled the wrong spot within a productive zone — perhaps you dug from the middle of the point bar instead of the downstream tail, or from the surface instead of near bedrock. If your first test pan is empty but the location looks good, try another pan from a slightly different spot within the same feature before ruling it out.

**Quantify your results.** Experienced prospectors often think in terms of "colors per pan" — how many visible gold particles are in each pan. One color per pan is a modest result; five colors per pan is encouraging; twenty or more per pan suggests a genuinely rich spot worth working thoroughly. These numbers vary a lot by location and season, but tracking them gives you a basis for comparing spots rather than just remembering vaguely that "this one seemed okay."

## Flood Gold vs. Low-Water Gold: The Seasonal Shift

The character of a stream changes dramatically between high water and low water, and so does where the gold is likely to be.

During high water, the creek moves with enormous energy. Material that normally sits still gets picked up and moved. When the flood recedes, it deposits material in a new pattern. **Flood gold** settles wherever the receding high water slowed down — which often means different places than low-water gold. High water pushes material onto banks and terraces that are dry in summer. It rearranges the point bars on inside bends. It buries some old concentrations under fresh overburden and creates new ones in unexpected places.

Prospectors who work the same stretch of water across multiple seasons often describe a kind of annual rhythm: after spring runoff, the system is reshuffled and new concentrations are accessible on the surface. By late summer, those surface concentrations have been worked by many hands, and productive digging requires going deeper or further afield.

At low water, you can reach parts of the streambed that are underwater in higher flows. Exposed gravel bars, mid-channel boulders, stretches of bedrock — all of these become accessible. Low-water prospecting is often more productive per spot simply because you can reach more of the creek, including bedrock sections that have been accumulating material undisturbed through every high-water season.

The ideal post-flood timing: wait until the creek has dropped to a safe and comfortable working level (clarity is returning, debris load is decreasing, current velocity is manageable), but don't wait so long that the surface deposits have been re-buried by subsequent flows. That window varies by drainage — some drop and clear within days, others take weeks. Learning the timing of your favorite water is part of building a long-term relationship with a particular stretch of creek.

## When the Creek Surprises You

Even experienced prospectors encounter stretches that look productive but yield nothing, and stretches that look unremarkable but are surprisingly rich. This happens for a few reasons.

**The gold has to get there first.** All the hydraulic sorting in the world doesn't help if the upstream geology isn't delivering gold to this part of the drainage. You can be standing in a textbook point bar on a creek that has essentially no placer gold in it because the geology upstream isn't producing any. Stream-reading gets you to the right spots within a gold-bearing drainage — it doesn't create gold where the geology doesn't support it.

**Historical disturbance matters.** A spot that looks perfect according to stream-reading principles may have been intensively worked during the Gold Rush or in subsequent decades. The 1849 rush and the decade that followed were extraordinarily thorough in many California drainages — hydraulic monitors moved hillsides; dredges worked entire valley floors. In historically well-mined areas, accessible spots have often been worked before. The most productive digging may require going deeper, further from road access, or into areas that were logistically difficult for historical miners.

**Layering in the gravel profile.** Gold doesn't always sit at the current bedrock surface — it can be stratified within the gravel profile at former bedrock surfaces or clay layers. A spot where surface material is barren may have a productive layer two or three feet down. This is why some experienced prospectors dig test holes rather than just scraping the surface — to look at the full profile.

## Reading Gets Faster With Practice

The first few times you apply these principles, it'll feel deliberate and slow. You'll be consciously thinking through each factor, talking yourself through the logic. That's fine. That's how it's supposed to feel.

After a dozen outings, it gets faster. You'll glance at a bend and immediately know which side of it to work. You'll see a boulder field and your eye will go straight to the downstream convergence points. You'll notice a line of darker gravel and walk toward it before you've consciously articulated why. You'll know from the look of the gravel profile whether you're near bedrock without digging.

That's stream-reading becoming instinct. It's a real skill, built from a simple principle applied to hundreds of different creek configurations. The principle doesn't change. The creek is always sorting by weight. Your job is to find where the sorting drops its heaviest material.

Get out enough times, and you'll stop wondering where to look. The water will tell you.
