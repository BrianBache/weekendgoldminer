## The Creek Is Telling You Something. Here's How to Listen.

The first time most people go gold prospecting, they pick a spot the same way they'd pick a place to sit at a picnic: whatever looks nice, whatever's close to where they parked, wherever there's a flat area to stand. Then they dig, and they wonder why they're not finding anything.

Experienced prospectors walk up to a creek they've never seen before and know within a few minutes where to start. They're not guessing. They're reading. The creek is showing them exactly where a heavy piece of gold would end up if one were moving through the system, and they've learned how to follow that logic.

This is one of the most useful skills in all of recreational prospecting, and it's almost entirely learnable from principles. You don't need years of experience to develop a working understanding of how water moves gold. You need to understand one main idea and see how it plays out in different situations. After that, it's pattern recognition — and pattern recognition gets faster every time you go out.

---

## The One Idea That Explains Everything

Water moving through a stream carries sediment. The faster the water moves, the heavier the material it can carry. When the water slows down, things fall out of suspension. The heaviest things fall first.

Gold is much heavier than everything else in a typical streambed — roughly seven times heavier than ordinary gravel. So the moment the current loses energy, gold drops. It doesn't travel a few more feet like lighter sand. It drops, immediately, right there.

Every technique for reading a stream is really just a technique for asking: where does this water slow down? Because wherever it slows down is where gold settles.

The creek is a machine that sorts material by weight every single time it runs. It's been doing it for thousands of years. Your job is to find the places where the machine deposits its heaviest cargo.

## Inside Bends: The Classic Starting Point

If you only ever learn one thing about reading a stream, learn this: gold concentrates on the inside of bends.

When a stream curves around a bend, the water on the outside of the curve moves faster — it has more distance to cover in the same amount of time. The water on the inside of the curve moves slower. Slower water drops material. Heavier material drops first.

The result is a classic pattern: on the outside of a bend, you'll often see the bank undercut and eroded, because fast-moving water is eating at it. On the inside of the bend, you'll see a gravel bar — a crescent of deposited material that the slower water couldn't keep moving. That gravel bar is where gold concentrates.

Walk up to any creek that curves, look at the inside of that curve, and you're looking at a natural gold trap. Not every gravel bar has gold — the gold has to get there first, and that depends on what's upstream — but the mechanics of deposition are always working. Inside bends are where you start.

In prospecting circles, this accumulation on the inside of a bend is sometimes called a **point bar**. You'll hear that term among experienced folks. Now you know what it means and why it matters.

## Downstream of Obstructions

Anything that slows the current creates an opportunity for gold to drop. Boulders are the most obvious example.

In front of a boulder — upstream — the water piles up and accelerates around the sides. Behind the boulder — downstream — there's a zone of slower, calmer water in the boulder's shadow. Gold moving downstream hits that slow zone and sinks. Over time, especially in higher flows when more material is moving, a lot of material can accumulate behind a large boulder.

But here's the part beginners often miss: the productive zone isn't always directly behind the boulder where you'd expect a calm eddy. Often it's further downstream, where the currents that split around the boulder reconnect and slow down. That reconnection point — called a **converging flow zone** — is where the water loses energy in a concentrated way. Work both areas: behind the boulder and a little further downstream where the current rejoins.

Logs, bridge pilings, anything that creates a break in the current follows the same logic. Any obstruction in a stream has a downstream shadow, and that shadow is worth investigating.

## Bedrock: The Floor of the Trap

Sand and gravel move. Bedrock doesn't. This distinction is critical.

Gold is heavy enough that it sinks through loose material over time. Given years of water movement and vibration — every spring flood, every period of high flow — gold works its way down through the gravel and sand until it hits something it can't work through anymore: bedrock.

This means the most concentrated gold in any streambed is often not scattered through the gravel. It's sitting in the cracks, pockets, and irregularities of the bedrock underneath. Prospectors have a word for the residue that accumulates in those bedrock crevices: **pay dirt**, used literally — the dirt that pays.

When you're working an area and you start to get down close to bedrock, pay attention. Collect the material from crevices and pockets carefully. Get a flat tool — a crevice tool or even a sturdy butter knife — and work the material out of cracks in the rock. This is often where your best panning material comes from, even if the gravel above it was mostly empty.

If you can see bedrock in a streambed — exposed by current, visible at low water — that's a good sign. It tells you the stream has been actively working through the sediment, and it gives you direct access to the layer where gold accumulates. Bedrock in the creek, especially bedrock with natural pockets, crevices, and cross-ribs, is always worth a closer look.

## Pay Attention to Color Changes in the Gravel

Not all streambed material is the same color, and the variation tells you something. Dark material — especially the black sand made of magnetite and other heavy iron minerals — travels with gold for the same reason gold concentrates where it does: it's heavier than ordinary sand and gravel, and it settles in the same slow-water zones.

Where black sand concentrates, gold concentrates. Not always — but often enough that learning to spot and follow black sand is one of the most reliable beginner habits in the hobby. When you're digging test holes to see what's in an area, the presence of heavy black sand in your pan is a good sign. Its absence doesn't guarantee nothing is there, but its presence means you're in an area where the stream is doing the right kind of sorting.

Color changes in the gravel can also indicate changes in the upstream geology — different rock types washing down from different tributaries or different parts of the drainage. Quartz — white or milky — is worth noticing, since gold-bearing hydrothermal veins often form in quartz. A streambed with a lot of quartz pebbles suggests you're in the kind of geology where gold veins may have formed nearby.

## Flood Gold vs. Low-Water Gold

The character of a stream changes dramatically between high water and low water, and so does where the gold is likely to be.

During high water — spring floods, major rain events — the creek moves with enormous energy. Material that normally sits still gets picked up and moved. Gold that had settled in one place gets redistributed. When the flood recedes, it deposits material in a new pattern, and some of that material includes gold in spots that weren't productive before.

**Flood gold** settles wherever the high water slowed down, which often means different places than where low-water gold is found. High water can push material up onto banks and terraces that are dry in summer. It can rearrange the inside-bend gravel bars. It can bury old concentrations and create new ones.

This is why prospectors often do well in an area right after a significant flood event — the system has been reshuffled, and new concentrations are sitting at the surface. But it also means that spots you've worked successfully before may be less productive after a big flood, and new spots may have opened up. Always be willing to test and reassess.

Low water is a different story. When the creek drops, you can access parts of the streambed that are underwater in higher flows. Exposed gravel bars, mid-channel boulders, stretches of bedrock — all of these become accessible. Low-water prospecting is often more productive simply because you can reach more of the creek, including spots that have been accumulating material undisturbed through every high-water season.

## How to Sample a New Area

When you arrive at a spot you haven't worked before, don't just pick a random location and start digging. Take a few minutes to read the water first.

Look for the inside bends. Look for boulders with calm downstream shadows. Look for any exposed bedrock, especially bedrock with crevices. Look for the darkest patches of gravel — the heaviest material will be visually distinct from the surrounding lighter sand.

Then start with quick test pans. A test pan is exactly what it sounds like: you fill your pan with material from a location, run it down, and see what's there. You're not trying to mine — you're trying to figure out whether the spot is worth working more thoroughly. Test pan a few of the likely-looking spots before committing to any one of them.

What you're looking for in a test pan: black sand (a good sign), the occasional metallic flake (a better sign), and — if you're in a productive spot — the tiny glint of gold settling into the bottom of the pan. Even a single small flake is worth noting. It tells you the gold is in this part of the drainage.

If your test pans are consistently empty — no black sand, no flakes, nothing heavy — consider moving upstream or downstream. You may be in the wrong section of the creek for the geology to be delivering gold to this stretch.

## Reading Gets Faster With Practice

The first few times you apply these principles, it'll feel deliberate and slow. You'll be consciously thinking through each factor, talking yourself through the logic. That's fine. That's how it's supposed to feel.

After a dozen outings, it gets faster. You'll glance at a bend and immediately know which side of it to work. You'll see a boulder field and your eye will go straight to the downstream reconnection points. You'll notice a line of darker gravel and walk toward it before you've consciously articulated why.

That's stream-reading becoming instinct. It's a real skill, built from a simple principle applied to a thousand different creek configurations. The principle doesn't change. The creek is always sorting by weight. Your job is to find where the sorting drops its heaviest material.

Get out enough times, and you'll stop wondering where to look. The water will tell you.
