> **Note:** This guide focuses on field ethics and best practices for recreational gold prospectors. References to legal and regulatory context are general in nature. Always verify current rules with the relevant land management agency before going out.

## The Access We Have Is Worth Protecting

Millions of acres of public land in the United States are open to recreational gold prospecting. On BLM land, in national forests, along countless streams and riverbeds, ordinary people can go out with a pan and a classifier and spend a day working the gravel — no mining permit, no expensive equipment, no special status required. That access exists because recreational panning, done correctly, has a minimal impact on the land.

That phrase — *done correctly* — carries a lot of weight.

The continued openness of public land to hobby prospectors depends on prospectors collectively behaving in a way that justifies continued access. When people leave gaping holes in the streambank, scatter tailings across a hundred feet of creek, disturb sensitive fish habitat, or crowd out other prospectors who've been working a section for hours, it creates pressure — from environmental groups, from other recreationists, from land management agencies — to restrict or eliminate access. And it has happened, in places where the community failed to hold itself to a reasonable standard.

This is the part of prospecting that doesn't get covered in the gear guides or the YouTube tutorials. But it matters as much as anything else in the hobby. No amount of stream-reading skill makes up for being the kind of prospector who leaves a mess behind.

## Fill Your Holes: The Non-Negotiable

This is the single most important practice in the field, and it's not optional.

When you dig a hole — in the bank, in the streambed, anywhere — you fill it back in before you leave. Every time. No exceptions.

**The erosion argument.** An open hole in a streambank is an erosion initiation point. Streambanks are held together by a combination of root systems, sediment cohesion, and the physical structure of the material. When you remove material and leave an open excavation, you've created a weak point. The next high-water event will focus erosion there, enlarging the hole, undermining the bank further, and sending excess sediment downstream. Excess fine sediment in a stream is a real ecological harm — it smothers fish eggs buried in spawning gravel and reduces oxygen exchange in the water column. Your unfilled hole becomes a long-term sediment source.

**The courtesy argument.** Other prospectors coming to an area after you shouldn't have to deal with your excavations. A hole in a bank is more than an eyesore — it's a sign that whoever dug it didn't respect the place or the people who come after. The prospecting community is small and its reputation matters. Every hole left open contributes to the image that prompts people to call their elected representatives and ask why hobby mining is allowed on public land.

**The legal argument.** Most land management rules — BLM, Forest Service, and state agencies — require that disturbed areas be reclaimed to approximate natural condition. For recreational panning, the practical interpretation is simple: fill your holes. This isn't formally enforced against solo panners most of the time, but it is the stated legal standard, and prospectors who visibly and repeatedly leave the land disturbed have contributed to regulatory tightening in multiple regions. The rules that exist today are partly the result of yesterday's thoughtless prospectors.

**The practice.** The habit is simple to develop. Before you leave any spot, take five minutes. Shovel your tailings back in the hole. Push the gravel back into the streambed disturbed area. Smooth out the bank face. Tamp it down loosely. You don't need it to be perfect — nature will take over and integrate the disturbed area into the surrounding material within a season or two if you've made a reasonable effort. You just need to make a genuine attempt.

If you're working a high bank — material significantly above the waterline — and you've removed a substantial volume, make sure the backfill is compacted enough that it won't slump in the next rain. Loose, piled-up tailings on a steep bank face will erode into the creek at the first opportunity. Compact as you go.

## Managing Tailings: Don't Let Them Become the Problem

When you classify and process material, you create tailings — the gravel and sand left after the gold has been removed. How you handle this material is a visible measure of how seriously you take your responsibility to the land.

**Don't pile tailings on the bank.** A high, concentrated pile of tailings left on a streambank is both an eyesore and an ecological problem. It will erode unevenly, washing into the creek in concentrated pulses during rains. It can block small animal access routes, change local drainage patterns, and serve as a visible target for complaints.

**Don't leave oversized rocks scattered in the streambed.** When you classify and remove large rocks from your working area, the natural instinct is to toss them wherever. But a scattered pile of large rocks in a streambed alters local flow patterns, can create artificial current structures, and looks exactly like what it is — someone's discarded material left behind. Return oversized rocks approximately where they came from, or disperse them in a way that doesn't alter the streambed character noticeably.

**The best practice is continuous restoration.** Work your tailings back into the area as you go rather than letting them accumulate. Some prospectors bring a folding work mat or tarp — they classify on the mat, and material that isn't going into the pan gets shoveled back toward the hole progressively. When they leave, there's relatively little restoration work because they've been doing it all along.

**Tailings from sluice operations** need special attention. A sluice running all day can process enormous volumes of material, and the tailings pile downstream of the sluice can become significant. Position your sluice so the tailings drop in an area of the stream that can naturally handle the additional sediment load, not on top of sensitive gravel beds or pools. Clean up the tailings pile before you leave.

## Never Use Mercury. Full Stop.

This bears explicit statement because mercury is still discussed in older prospecting literature, and some beginners encounter the concept without fully understanding what they're looking at.

**Mercury was historically used to amalgamate gold** — when liquid mercury contacts gold, the gold dissolves into the mercury, forming an amalgam. The amalgam was then heated, driving off the mercury vapor and leaving the gold. This technique was used extensively in the 19th century and into the 20th.

It was also an environmental catastrophe. Mercury from California's Gold Rush-era hydraulic and hard-rock mines contaminated rivers, valleys, and the San Francisco Bay estuary to levels that persist today, nearly 175 years later. The Sacramento River watershed still has mercury advisories on fish consumption because of 19th-century mining activity. Mercury bioaccumulates in the food chain — small organisms absorb it, fish eat those organisms and concentrate it further, and humans who eat fish from mercury-contaminated waters are exposed to significant health risks.

Do not use mercury. Do not bring it to the field. Do not experiment with it at home. It is acutely toxic, environmentally persistent, bioaccumulative, and illegal to discharge into waterways under federal law. Any older prospecting book that casually describes using mercury should be read with this filter clearly applied. The technique existed. It was damaging. It has no place in modern recreational prospecting.

## Waterway Protection: Understanding What You're Standing In

Streams are living systems. The water you're standing in supports fish, macroinvertebrates (the insects and crustaceans at the base of the aquatic food chain), amphibians, riparian vegetation, and the entire cascade of wildlife that depends on those things. Recreational prospecting with hand tools has minimal impact when done thoughtfully. It can have real impact when done carelessly.

**Spawning habitat deserves special care.** Salmon, steelhead, trout, and many other native fish species bury their eggs in stream gravel beds called **redds**. The female digs a depression in the gravel, deposits eggs, and covers them — the eggs incubate in the gravel for weeks, depending on water temperature, receiving oxygenated water through the interstitial spaces between gravel particles. If you disturb that gravel — digging it up, running material through a sluice on top of an active redd, walking heavily through it — you can crush eggs directly or compact the gravel enough to reduce the oxygen flow that keeps the eggs alive.

Even outside formal closure periods, be aware of the possibility of redds. Redds often appear as oval, cleaned patches of gravel — distinctly lighter and cleaner than surrounding material because the female has removed the silt and algae from the surface. If you see what might be a redd, move away from it. The fish that built it traveled hundreds of miles upstream to find exactly that spot. Give it room.

**Keep your footprint small.** Work one area at a time rather than spreading disturbed material across a wide stretch of bank or streambed. The more concentrated your work, the easier it is to restore the area before you leave, and the smaller the biological impact. Resist the temptation to "just check one more spot" if it means trampling another 20 feet of bank.

**Contaminant awareness goes beyond mercury.** Don't let fuel, oil, or lubricants from any equipment near the water. Don't dump anything — food waste, cleaning products, solvents, anything — into or near the creek. Keep your work zone clean. Carry your trash out, including all the plastic packaging from your equipment, snack wrappers, and anything else that came out of your pack.

**Invasive species** are a real concern that most prospectors never think about. Moving from one watershed to another with muddy, wet equipment can transfer invasive aquatic organisms — plants, invertebrates, pathogens — between drainages. New Zealand mudsnail (Potamopyrgus antipodarum) is a tiny, nearly invisible invasive snail that has devastated aquatic ecosystems across the western United States; it spreads primarily through wet equipment moved between waterways. Felt-soled waders were banned in many states for this reason (felt is nearly impossible to fully decontaminate). Clean your equipment — waders, boots, pans, sluice boxes — before moving between drainages. Let them dry completely, or use approved decontamination methods (freezing, dilute bleach solution, or hot water above 140°F). This isn't an arcane concern — it's one of the more significant ecological risks associated with any water-based outdoor activity.

## Respect Other Prospectors: the Social Code

The prospecting community is, in general, a generous and friendly one. People share spots, share technique, help beginners figure out their panning stroke, and look out for each other in the field. Maintaining that culture requires everyone to contribute to it.

**Space and territory.** The basic courtesies are obvious but worth stating: don't crowd someone who's working a spot. If another prospector is working a section of creek — and it's clear from their equipment, their test holes, and their pattern of work that they're systematically covering that section — don't move in and start working the same stretch. Give people room. In a popular area, the general rule is to set up at least 100 feet from another active prospector unless there's explicit agreement otherwise.

**If you're not sure, ask.** Prospectors communicate directly and the conversation is almost always friendly. "Hey, are you working down to that boulder?" is a completely normal exchange at the creek. Most people will tell you exactly what stretch they're planning to work and where they're done. This takes 30 seconds and prevents the awkward situation of someone feeling encroached upon.

**Mining claims.** This deserves special emphasis because it's not always obvious. An active mining claim can look, from the creek, exactly like open public land. There may be no fence, no sign you can easily see, no rope marking the boundary. It's your responsibility to know what claims exist in the area where you're prospecting — not the claim holder's responsibility to make it obvious to casual visitors. Respect for other prospectors includes respecting the rights of claim holders, whether or not you've met them. We cover how to research claims in the legal guide.

**Club areas.** Many prospecting clubs — the Gold Prospectors Association of America (GPAA) and its regional chapters, state-level clubs, and informal local groups — secure access to specific sections of creek for their members through agreements with claim holders or land managers. If you're in a known club area, understand the access conditions and follow posted rules. Club members work hard to maintain these relationships. Visitors who ignore the rules threaten access for everyone who belongs.

**Sharing finds.** If you've found a productive spot and you're done for the day, consider sharing that information with the community — at least in broad terms. "The downstream side of that big boulder at the third bend has some nice color" is the kind of tip that keeps the community knowledge growing and builds goodwill. You don't have to give away your exact honey hole, and nobody expects you to. But the culture of generous knowledge-sharing that characterizes the hobby doesn't maintain itself — it requires participants to contribute to it.

## Interacting with the Public and Landowners

When you're prospecting on public land, you may still encounter private property nearby — agricultural land, ranches, homes, private access roads. The distinction between public and private land isn't always obvious on the ground, and most conflicts that arise between prospectors and the broader public start with some version of "I didn't know I was on private land."

**When in doubt, find out before you dig.** If you're near a property boundary you're not sure about, err on the side of caution. Return to your vehicle, check your maps, and confirm the land status before going back to work. Trespassing — even unintentional trespassing — creates tension, calls to law enforcement, and bad community relations that ripple out beyond your individual trip.

**Be an ambassador.** When you're at a public recreation area or trailhead with your pan and classifier, you represent the hobby to every hiker, angler, and picnicking family who walks by. How you conduct yourself shapes how they think about prospectors. If you're friendly, clean up after yourself, and leave the spot better than you found it, you've done something for every person who comes to that stream after you.

**When someone asks what you're doing**, be straight with them. Explain the hobby simply and honestly. Most people are curious rather than hostile, and a brief friendly conversation often turns into genuine interest. The hobby has a way of attracting people when they understand what it actually involves. You have an opportunity to be a good representative of the community every time someone stops to watch.

**Campfire and camping ethics** apply if you're making a multi-day trip. In riparian zones — the vegetated buffer along waterways — fire risk and ecological sensitivity are typically high. Follow all fire restrictions, use existing fire rings when available, and err strongly toward no fire when conditions are dry or restrictions are in effect. Pack out all waste. The "leave no trace" ethic applies in full at water sources.

## The Unwritten Rule That Runs Through All of This

Experienced prospectors have a saying that gets repeated often enough to qualify as community gospel: *the gold belongs to everyone until you find it, and the land belongs to everyone always.*

What that means in practice: you get to keep what you find, but the land isn't yours to damage, privatize, or degrade. The access that makes this hobby possible is a shared resource. The streams are shared. The bedrock that accumulates gold is shared. The public land that frames it all is held in trust for every American.

Treat it that way, and it stays open — for you, for the families coming out next weekend, and for the people who'll be doing this long after you're gone.

The ethics of prospecting aren't complicated. They mostly reduce to this: don't be careless, don't be selfish, and leave it better than you found it. Do that consistently, and you'll never have cause to worry about anything else.
