## Something Caught Your Eye — Now What?

You're working your way through a pan of concentrate and something doesn't look like sand. It's catching the light differently. You slow down, tilt the pan, and there it is — something metallic, something that wasn't supposed to be there.

Before you start planning what to do with your fortune, you need to figure out what you're actually looking at. Natural metal deposits show up in creek beds in more forms than most beginners expect, and the differences between real gold and its look-alikes can be subtle if you don't know what to watch for.

This guide walks you through the metals you're most likely to encounter as a prospector — what they look like, how they behave, and the field tests you can run on the spot without a chemistry degree or a laboratory.

---

## Gold: What You're Looking For

Gold has a specific gravity of 19.3 g/cm³. That's not just heavy — it's nearly eight times heavier than ordinary creek sand and more than three times heavier than iron. In practical terms: if it's the same size as a grain of quartz and it stays in the pan when everything else has washed away, that weight is telling you something.

**Color:** Natural placer gold is a warm, buttery yellow. Not bright, not flashy — warm. The color is consistent whether you're looking at it in bright sunlight or shade. This is key: real gold doesn't shift color when you move it.

**Luster:** Gold glows rather than flashes. Mica flashes as light hits its flat surfaces at changing angles. Pyrite (fool's gold) produces a sharp, angular glint. Gold reflects light evenly, from any angle. Tilt the pan. Rotate it. The color stays.

**Malleability:** Gold is one of the most malleable metals on earth. If you pick up a suspected flake and press it between two smooth rocks, or flatten it with a thumbnail, real gold will deform — it'll squash rather than shatter. This single test rules out most impostors. Pyrite is brittle and will crumble or crack. Mica is flaky and will break apart.

**Tarnish:** Gold doesn't tarnish. Ever. No oxidation, no patina, no color change. A piece of gold that's been sitting in a streambed for a thousand years looks the same as the day it arrived.

## Pyrite: The Classic Impostors

Pyrite (iron sulfide, FeS₂) is the most common source of false hope for new prospectors. It's gold-colored, heavy relative to ordinary sand, and shows up in the same geological environments as real gold. The frustration is built in.

But pyrite and gold behave completely differently under any kind of testing:

**The streak test:** Find a piece of unglazed ceramic — the back of a bathroom tile works perfectly. Drag your sample across it firmly. Real gold leaves a gold-yellow streak. Pyrite leaves a greenish-black or black streak. That streak test takes three seconds and gives you an immediate answer.

**The hardness test:** Gold has a Mohs hardness of about 2.5 — it's soft, and a thumbnail (hardness ~2.5) can barely scratch it. Pyrite has a hardness of 6–6.5 and will not be scratched by a fingernail or knife blade — it'll scratch the knife. If something in your pan is resisting your fingernail, that's a bad sign for gold.

**Shape:** Placer gold tends to be rounded, irregular, and flat — shaped by thousands of years of tumbling and abrasion in the streambed. Pyrite shows cubic crystal faces and sharp edges. If what you found has geometric facets and sharp right-angle edges, it's pyrite.

**Specific gravity:** Gold is 19.3. Pyrite is about 5.0 — still heavy, but not in the same class. If you have a way to estimate relative weight (putting the sample in water and comparing its displacement to a known material), the difference is enormous.

When in doubt: streak test, then malleability. Those two together will resolve nearly every gold vs. pyrite question you encounter in the field.

## Silver: The Other Noble Metal

Silver shows up in some prospecting areas, usually associated with certain geological formations rather than the placer environments where gold is most common. It's not a frequent find in a creek pan, but it's worth knowing.

**Appearance:** Fresh silver is bright, mirror-white, and very shiny. The problem is that silver tarnishes readily — exposure to air, water, and sulfur compounds turns it black or dark gray. A piece of silver that's been in a streambed for any significant time will be darkened, mottled, or coated. If you find something heavy and dark gray that turns out to have a bright metallic layer underneath when scratched, silver is worth considering.

**Weight:** Silver has a specific gravity of 10.5 — heavier than iron but significantly less dense than gold. It'll stay in the pan through most of the wash cycle, but it won't hug the center the way gold does.

**Streak test:** Silver leaves a silver-white to gray streak on unglazed ceramic.

Silver in native (pure metal) form is genuinely rare as a placer find. Most silver-bearing material in creek beds is silver-containing ore — argentite or other compounds — rather than metallic silver itself.

## Platinum and Palladium: Heavy, Rare, and Easily Missed

Platinum and palladium are both platinum-group metals (PGMs). They show up in specific geological contexts — associated with certain ultramafic rock types — and are genuinely uncommon as placer finds, but not impossible.

**Platinum:** Silver-white to steel-gray in color. Doesn't tarnish. Resists almost all chemical attack. The defining characteristic is its weight: platinum has a specific gravity of 21.4 — heavier than gold. If you have something that looks like a dull gray-white metal, doesn't tarnish, and is absurdly heavy for its size, platinum is a real possibility. It's been found in placer operations in California, Oregon, and Alaska, where the right ultrabasic geology is present.

**Palladium:** Similar appearance to platinum — silver-white, non-tarnishing — but with a specific gravity of about 12.0, significantly lighter than platinum. It's even rarer in placer form than platinum.

Both metals are worth significantly more per troy ounce than gold at current prices. If you think you have a PGM, don't do field tests beyond visual inspection and weight estimation — take it to an assay office.

## Copper: Common, Pretty, and Worth Knowing

Copper is one of the more commonly encountered metals in creek beds, particularly in areas with copper-bearing geology. It's not valuable in small amounts, but it's interesting and easy to identify.

**Appearance:** Fresh copper is a distinctive reddish-orange. You'd recognize it immediately — it looks like a new penny. However, copper that's been exposed to water and air oxidizes to a green patina (verdigris, copper carbonate). You might find a reddish-orange piece, or a greenish piece that reveals copper underneath a scratch.

**Weight:** Copper's specific gravity is 8.96 — heavy enough to stay in your pan through most of the washing process, but not nearly as heavy as gold.

**Streak test:** Copper leaves a copper-red streak.

Finding copper in a creek can be a useful geological indicator. Copper and gold sometimes occur together in porphyry deposits, and copper in a stream drainage may suggest the presence of related mineralization worth investigating further upstream.

## Black Sand: Not Metal, But Important

Black sand will be the most common heavy material in your pan. Understanding it properly is as important as knowing what gold looks like.

Black sand is primarily **magnetite** (Fe₃O₄), with some hematite and other iron minerals. It has a specific gravity of about 5.2 — far heavier than quartz sand (2.65), which is why it sorts with the heavies in your pan.

**The magnet test:** Pass a rare-earth magnet (in a plastic bag, to avoid having sand stick to the magnet directly) through your black sand. Magnetite is strongly magnetic and will cling to the bag. Real gold is non-magnetic and won't move. This is one of the cleanest field separations you can do — magneting out the magnetite often reveals what's actually left.

Black sand is also your best friend as an indicator: where the water is doing genuine heavy-mineral sorting, black sand concentrates. Where black sand concentrates, gold — if it's in the area — concentrates too.

## Field Tests You Can Do Without a Lab

To summarize the practical toolkit:

**Streak test** — Unglazed ceramic tile. Drag the sample firmly across it. Gold leaves a gold streak. Pyrite leaves black-green. Silver leaves gray-white. Iron minerals (magnetite, hematite) leave black to red-brown.

**Magnet test** — Rare-earth magnet in a plastic bag. Pulls magnetite and hematite strongly. Pulls pyrrhotite (another iron mineral) weakly. Does nothing to gold, silver, platinum, or copper.

**Malleability test** — Press or tap the sample with something smooth and hard. Gold deforms. Pyrite shatters. Mica flakes apart. Copper deforms but has a more springy feel than gold.

**Visual test** — Color consistency under different lighting angles. Warmth of color. Presence of crystal faces (suggests pyrite, not gold).

**Acid test:** Muriatic acid (hydrochloric acid) reacts with most metals and many minerals — but not gold. A drop on a suspected gold sample: if it fizzes, it's not gold. Gold is also resistant to nitric acid. Professional assayers use aqua regia (a mixture of both). If you're doing acid testing in the field: wear gloves, eye protection, and don't mix acids. This test is more safely done at home or in a controlled setting. For field work, the streak and malleability tests are safer and usually sufficient.

## When to Take It to an Assay Office

Field tests are good for making reasonable identifications. They are not definitive, and they won't give you accurate weight or purity measurements.

Take your material to an assay office when:

- You have a meaningful accumulation of gold you want to sell or store
- You've found something unusual that doesn't fit the common categories cleanly
- You suspect you have platinum-group metals
- You want official documentation of what you found and its value

Assay offices exist in most gold-country states. A basic fire assay for gold takes a few days and runs $30–$80 depending on the office and what analysis you're requesting. Some dealers and coin shops offer simpler (less precise) tests for free if you're looking to sell. For anything more than a small amount of gold, the assay is worth the cost — it tells you what you actually have, in precise terms, before any transaction.

The hobby is built on observation and honest assessment. Learn to see what's in your pan accurately, test what you find with the tools you have, and know when to step it up to a professional evaluation. That's the whole sequence, and it gets faster and more intuitive with every trip.
