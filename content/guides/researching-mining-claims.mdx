There's a version of this story that ends badly, and it starts innocently enough: you find a promising stretch of creek on Google Maps, you drive out on a Saturday morning, you set up your sluice box, and a few hours later someone walks up and tells you you're on their claim.

Best case, they're patient about it. They explain, you pack up, you leave embarrassed. Worst case, you're looking at a federal trespass complaint, a fine, and a very bad day.

Most people in that situation — and it happens more than you'd think — genuinely didn't know the claim was there. That's understandable. Mining claims are invisible from the road. They're not always well-marked. And nobody hands you a map when you start getting into gold prospecting.

But here's the thing: the research to avoid it takes about five minutes. Every active mining claim filed with the Bureau of Land Management is in a public database you can search for free, right now, from your phone or laptop. You just have to know it exists.

This guide is going to walk you through the whole process — what claims look like on the ground, how to look them up before you go, and what to do if you stumble across one in the field.

---

## Why This Is a Bigger Deal Than It Sounds

Let's be straight about the stakes, because this isn't just a matter of being polite.

Under federal law, a valid unpatented mining claim grants the claimant the exclusive right to extract minerals from that ground. If you go in and work a claimed area without permission, you can be liable under multiple legal theories — trespass, conversion of minerals, interference with mining rights. The BLM and the Department of the Interior take this seriously. So does the claimant who's been paying annual maintenance fees to hold that ground.

Criminal prosecution for a first-time, clearly unintentional violation is rare. But civil liability for the value of any material you removed? That's very real. And federal trespass charges are not something you want to explain to an employer.

None of this is meant to scare you out of prospecting. The vast majority of public land in the western United States is open to recreational mining with a simple gold pan or small suction dredge, no claim required. The goal is to make sure you're on the right ground before you get there — and that five minutes of research is all it takes.

## What Mining Claims Look Like in the Field

Before we get into the database side of things, it helps to know what you're looking for on the ground, because claims don't always announce themselves clearly.

**Corner posts and boundary markers** are the primary physical indicators of a mining claim. By law, claimants are required to mark the boundaries of their claims with posts or monuments at each corner. These are usually wooden stakes — often a 4x4 post — driven into the ground, sometimes with a metal tag or placard attached. You'll also see rock cairns used in rocky terrain where stakes won't go in easily.

**Claim notice tubes** are one of the clearest field signs. These are typically a metal pipe or PVC tube attached to a corner post or nearby tree, capped at the top to keep weather out. Inside is a written claim notice — a document that identifies the claim name, the claimant's name, the case number, and the location. If you see one of these, you are looking at a marked mining claim. Stop what you're doing and read it.

**Stenciled posts and signs** are common but not universal. Many claimants will stencil or paint "MINING CLAIM — NO TRESPASSING" or similar language on their corner posts. This is helpful, but the absence of signage doesn't mean there's no claim. Some markers are old, faded, or missing entirely — the claim can still be legally valid.

**What to do if you find markers mid-trip:**

1. Stop work immediately. Do not collect any additional material.
2. Don't panic, but do take it seriously. Step back from the area you've been working.
3. Document what you saw — photograph the post, the notice tube, any written materials. Note the GPS coordinates if you can.
4. Leave the area. If you've already collected material, the honest move is to leave it behind or consult with an attorney before deciding what to do with it.
5. If the markers appear to be improperly placed (fake claim notices, harassment tactics with no actual BLM filing behind them — it does happen), note the details and report them to your local BLM field office. They take fraudulent claim posting seriously too.

You are not required to know every claim boundary in an area before you go — that's what the research step is for. But if you're in the field and you see corner posts or a claim notice tube, treat it as a hard stop.

## LR2000: The BLM's Public Mining Claim Database

The single most important tool in your pre-trip research kit is **LR2000**, the Bureau of Land Management's Land and Mineral Legacy Rehost 2000 system. That name tells you nothing useful, but the site does: it's a searchable database of every mining claim ever filed with the BLM, going back decades.

**The URL is:** [lr2000.blm.gov](https://lr2000.blm.gov)

It's free. No account required. And it covers all BLM-administered land in the United States.

### How to Search LR2000 Step by Step

When you land on the LR2000 homepage, you'll see a navigation menu with several options. You want **"Case Inquiry."** That's your main search interface.

**Step 1: Select your state.** The database is organized by state first. Choose the state where your target area is located.

**Step 2: Choose your county.** Within the state, you can narrow by county. If you know roughly where you're headed, this is the most efficient filter. Results at the county level can run into the thousands for mineral-rich areas, so you'll want to refine further.

**Step 3: Filter by case type.** For mining claims, you want to select **"Mining Claims"** as the case type. This filters out unrelated land cases (rights-of-way, leases, etc.) and focuses on what you need.

**Step 4: Filter by status.** This is critical. LR2000 tracks both active and closed/canceled claims. You want to see **active claims** — those coded as "Authorized" or "Pending" in the status field. Closed, canceled, or rejected claims are also in the database but represent ground that has reverted to open status (with some caveats we'll cover later).

**Step 5: Review your results.** Each result will show you:
- **Serial number / Case number** — the BLM's internal identifier for the claim
- **Claim name** — whatever the claimant named it (often something like "Lucky Gulch #1" or the claimant's last name)
- **Claimant name** — who holds the claim
- **Acreage** — how large the claim is (lode claims are typically 20 acres; placer claims can be up to 160 acres for an association placer)
- **Legal description** — the township, range, and section location in the Public Land Survey System
- **Status and dates** — when it was filed, when maintenance was last paid, current status

**Reading the map view:** LR2000 has a basic map interface that lets you see claim polygons overlaid on a map background. It's not pretty, and it's not the most intuitive thing in the world, but it works. Zoom to your target area and look for colored polygons — those are claims. Click on one and you'll pull up the case details.

Don't skip the map view. A county-level text search might return hundreds of results, and cross-referencing legal descriptions against your target location is slow work. The map cuts right to what you need.

### A Note on LR2000's Limitations

LR2000 is the authoritative federal record, but it has a few quirks to be aware of:

- **Updates can lag.** The database is updated regularly but not in real time. A claim filed very recently might not appear yet. When in doubt, call the local BLM field office and ask directly.
- **State-level claims are not here.** In states where mining claims can be filed at the state level (rather than or in addition to federally), those will be in a separate state database. More on that in a moment.
- **Location descriptions take practice.** If you're not familiar with the Public Land Survey System (townships, ranges, sections), the text results can be hard to parse. Use the map view.

## BLM Maps and GeoPDFs

LR2000 is your starting point, but it works best alongside official BLM field office maps for your target area.

The BLM produces **Surface Management Status maps** for every field office across the country. These show land ownership and management status — BLM land, National Forest, state land, private, tribal, etc. Before you go anywhere, you should have the relevant map downloaded or printed.

**Where to find them:** Start at [blm.gov/programs/lands-and-realty/cadastral-survey](https://www.blm.gov/programs/lands-and-realty/cadastral-survey) or search "BLM [state] surface management status map." Many field offices also post these directly on their local BLM office page.

The BLM also produces **GeoPDF maps** — georeferenced PDFs that work with apps like Avenza Maps. You can download a GeoPDF for your area, load it into Avenza on your phone, and then see your GPS position plotted directly on the BLM map in the field. No signal required once it's downloaded. This is genuinely useful for field navigation.

**Overlaying claim data:** The BLM's [National Integrated Land System (NILS) mapper](https://blm.maps.arcgis.com) and the BLM GeoBrowser allow you to layer mining claim data on top of topo and satellite backgrounds. These interfaces have more visual depth than LR2000's built-in map and are worth exploring if you want a clearer spatial picture of what's claimed in your target area.

## State-Specific Databases: Don't Forget Your State Agency

Here's something a lot of beginners don't know: the federal BLM database only covers mining claims filed under federal law on BLM-administered land. Many states have their own mineral rights filing systems, and those records live somewhere completely separate.

**Some examples:**
- **Montana:** The Montana Department of Natural Resources and Conservation (DNRC) maintains records for state-land mining claims. Check [dnrc.mt.gov](https://dnrc.mt.gov) for their public land records.
- **Idaho:** The Idaho Department of Lands handles state-administered mineral leasing. [idl.idaho.gov](https://www.idl.idaho.gov)
- **California:** The California State Mining and Geology Board and the Department of Conservation are relevant for state-land activity. California also has extensive private land with severed mineral rights.
- **Colorado:** The Colorado Division of Reclamation, Mining and Safety (DRMS) maintains permit records at [mining.colorado.gov](https://mining.colorado.gov)
- **Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico:** Each has a state agency equivalent. Google "[your state] DNRC" or "[your state] department of mining" to find yours.

**The bottom line:** Before your trip, run LR2000 for federal claims AND check your state's equivalent agency for state-land records. They're separate systems and neither one covers the other.

Also keep in mind: some gold-bearing areas sit on **private land** where mineral rights may be held separately from surface rights. That's a different research problem entirely — and one that requires checking county property records or title reports, not LR2000.

## Other Tools Worth Knowing About

The research landscape has improved a lot in the last few years thanks to some third-party tools that layer mining claim data onto better map interfaces.

**onX Maps** ([onxmaps.com](https://www.onxmaps.com)) — Originally built for hunters, onX has become a genuinely useful tool for prospectors too. Their land ownership layers show private vs. public land clearly, and they've added mining claim overlays sourced from BLM data. The mobile app works offline with downloaded maps. Subscription required, but their hunt app pricing is reasonable.

**ArcGIS / ESRI's BLM Map Layers** — The BLM publishes its data through ESRI's ArcGIS platform, and you can access BLM map layers (including mining claims) through ArcGIS Online for free. The learning curve is steeper than consumer apps, but the data is directly from BLM's systems and can be more current than third-party aggregators.

**Gold Prospectors Association of America (GPAA) maps** — If you're a GPAA member, their resource library includes claim maps for their club claims. Useful if you're prospecting GPAA-associated ground specifically.

**Limitations to know:**
- Third-party apps aggregate BLM data but don't always update in sync with LR2000. Never rely on a third-party app as your *only* check.
- App data may lag by weeks or months in areas with active claim turnover.
- Treat apps as a visual supplement to LR2000, not a replacement for it.

Use these tools to get a visual picture of what you're looking at. Use LR2000 to verify status. Use your state agency database for state-land areas. That three-source approach covers most of what you need.

## The Gray Zone: Lapsed and Closed Claims

This is where it gets a little murky, so pay attention.

LR2000 shows **both active and closed claims**. A claim that shows as "Closed," "Canceled," or "Null and Void" is no longer valid — meaning the claimant lost their rights to that ground, and it has reverted to open federal land (assuming everything else about the land is open to mining). In theory, you can legally prospect on closed-claim ground.

**But don't assume. Verify.**

Here's why the gray zone is tricky:

1. **Claims lapse for various reasons.** The most common is failure to pay the annual maintenance fee ($165 per claim as of recent BLM fee schedules). But sometimes a claimant lets a claim lapse, intending to re-file it. In the window between lapse and re-filing, the database may not reflect the new filing yet.

2. **The claimant may still be present on the ground.** Even after a claim closes, the claimant's equipment, infrastructure, or physical markers may still be in place. Their presence doesn't mean the claim is still valid — but it's worth confirming the database status before you assume the ground is open.

3. **Closed in LR2000 ≠ Open for Mining.** Some areas have additional restrictions — wilderness status, stream protection orders, environmental closures — that make them off-limits regardless of claim status. Check the applicable BLM or Forest Service land use plan for your area.

**The right move with any closed-claim ground:** Confirm the closed status in LR2000, note the case number and closure date, and if you're uncertain, call the local BLM field office before you go. A five-minute phone call is cheaper than a dispute.

## Quick Pre-Trip Checklist

Here's the five-step research routine you should run before every new prospecting location. Print this out if it helps.

**1. Confirm public land access.**
Open Google Maps or CalTopo and verify that your target location is actually on BLM or National Forest land, not state or private. Land ownership boundaries aren't always obvious.

**2. Search LR2000 for active claims.**
Go to [lr2000.blm.gov](https://lr2000.blm.gov). Search your state and county, filter for active mining claims, and check the map view to see if your target area overlaps with any claimed ground.

**3. Check your state agency database.**
If your target area includes state-administered land, check your state's DNRC or equivalent for state-level mineral filings.

**4. Download the BLM surface management map for your area.**
Get the relevant field office map (PDF or GeoPDF) and confirm your route and prospecting area are on open BLM land with no special closures.

**5. Call the local BLM field office if you're uncertain.**
If anything in your research is ambiguous — a claim that's close but might not overlap, a status that's unclear, an area you can't positively ID as open — call the field office. They're usually helpful, and this takes ten minutes.

That's it. Five steps, most of which you can knock out in a single browser session. If everything checks out, you go with confidence. If something flags, you know about it before you load the truck.

## One Last Thing

None of this is meant to discourage you from getting out there. The western United States has millions of acres of public land open to recreational gold prospecting, and most of it is gorgeous, accessible, and genuinely productive for someone with a pan and a few hours.

The point of research isn't to find reasons not to go. It's to go smart. Knowing the ground before you get there means you spend your Saturday finding gold instead of packing up early and driving home red-faced.

Five minutes of research. It's worth it every time.
